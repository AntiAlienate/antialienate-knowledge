# New Zealand Lawyers — Parental Alienation Practitioner Directory

**Schema version:** 1.0
**Last updated:** 2026-05-25
**License:** CC BY 4.0 (intended for github.com/AntiAlienate/antialienate-knowledge)
**Scope:** New Zealand-admitted barristers, solicitors, King's Counsel, retired judges and legal academics who have (a) appeared as named counsel in a reported PA decision, (b) published in the field, (c) sat on relevant working groups / Family Court reviews / Family Violence Death Review Committee, or (d) publicly stated a sourced stance on parental alienation in NZ family courts. Retired judges and legal academics without current practising certificates are included alongside practitioners because in NZ the published academic and policy-review literature (Te Korowai Ture-a-Whānau 2019; Leonetti 2022-2025; Tolmie / FVDRC) is the dominant load-bearing reference for any PA discussion at apex level.

**Standards:** All entries verified from primary sources (NZ Law Society / NZ Bar Association register, firm / chambers / faculty website, NZLII / Courts of NZ judgment portal, named press, peer-reviewed publications). Where a fact could not be confirmed from a public primary source it is flagged "**not publicly confirmed**".

**Stance taxonomy:**
- `recognition` — treats PA / alienating behaviour as a legitimate, litigable phenomenon worth pursuing in family court
- `critique` — argues PA is misused in family court, especially against DV / family-violence survivors
- `middle` — differentiated approach engaging both family-violence and alienating-behaviour frames; aligned with the post-2019 Te Korowai Ture-a-Whānau "child-centred + safety-primary" line

**Tier:** 1 = primary outreach (directly central to the NZ PA case-law / reform debate); 2 = secondary (relevant practice or institutional voice); 3 = background reference only.

**Note on jurisdiction:** New Zealand has a unitary family-law system. The Family Court sits as a specialist division of the District Court; appeals lie to the High Court, Court of Appeal and Supreme Court (decisions on NZLII at https://www.nzlii.org). Admission is governed by the New Zealand Law Society — Te Kāhui Ture o Aotearoa (https://www.lawsociety.org.nz/), with specialist practice support from the NZLS Family Law Section (https://www.lawsociety.org.nz/branches-sections-and-groups/family-law-section/). The doctrinal backdrop is the Care of Children Act 2004 and the Family Violence Act 2018, set against the 2019 Te Korowai Ture-a-Whānau independent panel report (https://www.justice.govt.nz/assets/family-justice-reforms-te-korowai-ture-a-whanau.pdf) which underpins the current phased reform programme.
