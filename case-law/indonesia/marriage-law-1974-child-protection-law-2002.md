# Indonesia Marriage Law 1974 + Child Protection Law 2002 — Non-Hague Complication

## TL;DR

Indonesia is the world's fourth-most-populous country (~280M) and the largest Muslim-majority democracy. Family law combines the **Marriage Law (UU No. 1 Tahun 1974)** governing civil marriage; the **Child Protection Law (UU No. 23 Tahun 2002, amended 2014)** governing children's rights; and the **Compilation of Islamic Law (KHI)** governing Muslim family matters via Religious Courts (Pengadilan Agama). **Indonesia is NOT a Hague 1980 signatory** — a major complication for cross-border PA and abduction cases. Article 41 Marriage Law and Article 14 Child Protection Law create the framework for custody and contact disputes, but enforcement against systematic obstruction is weaker than in Hague-member jurisdictions.

## Statutory Framework

### Marriage Law (UU No. 1 Tahun 1974)

#### Article 41 — Post-Divorce Parental Responsibility
After divorce, both father and mother retain the obligation to maintain and educate their children, based on the child's interests. If a dispute arises about custody (hak asuh), the court decides.

#### Article 45 — Parental Duties
Both parents must maintain and educate their children. Parental obligations continue until the child reaches majority or marries.

### Child Protection Law (UU No. 23 Tahun 2002 + UU No. 35 Tahun 2014)

#### Article 14 — Right to Care from Both Parents
The child has the right to be cared for by their parents — unless there is a legitimate reason and/or rule of law that demonstrates separation is in the child's best interests.

#### Article 14(2) (2014 amendment) — Right to Contact
Where parents are separated, the child has the right to meet directly and have a relationship personally with both parents, and obtain love, attention, security, and care from both. The 2014 amendment strengthened this contact right.

#### Article 26 — Parents' Obligations
Parents are obligated to: care for, raise, educate, and protect the child; nurture them in accordance with their abilities, talents, and interests; prevent marriage during childhood; provide character education and inculcate noble values.

### Compilation of Islamic Law (KHI) — Muslim Parties

For Muslim parties (~87% of Indonesians), Pengadilan Agama (Religious Courts) handles divorce and incidental custody matters under the KHI:
- Article 105: Custody of children under 12 (mumayyiz) generally awarded to mother absent contraindication
- Article 156: Custody arrangements + visitation rights
- Sharia-based age and developmental considerations apply

### Pancasila Foundational Principles
Indonesian constitutional jurisprudence is grounded in Pancasila (the five state principles), including "just and civilized humanity" — invoked in family-law cases to inform best-interests reasoning.

## Mahkamah Agung Jurisprudence

### MA RI Putusan 308 K/AG/2017
Indonesian Supreme Court (Religious Chamber) affirmed that systematic obstruction of the non-custodial parent's contact with the child is contrary to the child's best interests and grounds for custody modification under the KHI framework. Court must independently assess child's contact refusal.

### MK Putusan 71/PUU-XIV/2016 (Constitutional Court)
Constitutional Court held that both parents retain rights and duties regarding children post-divorce, including the right to contact. Limited interpretations of Article 41 Marriage Law were rejected.

### Pre-2014 vs Post-2014 Practice
The 2014 amendment to the Child Protection Law strengthened contact-rights framing. Post-2014 Indonesian jurisprudence increasingly recognizes systematic alienation as a child-welfare concern, though doctrinal development lags behind Common Law and European jurisdictions.

## Non-Hague Complication — Cross-Border PA Cases

Indonesia is one of the world's largest non-Hague-1980 jurisdictions. Practical implications for cross-border PA cases:

### Hague Return NOT Available
If a child is wrongfully retained in Indonesia, the targeted parent CANNOT invoke Hague return procedures. They must:
- Litigate in Indonesian courts under Indonesian family law (slow + uncertain)
- Negotiate via diplomatic channels (typically ineffective)
- Pursue extradition or criminal-law remedies if applicable (rare)

### Bilateral Cooperation
Indonesia has limited bilateral mutual-recognition agreements. Some progress with Australia (significant cross-border families), Singapore, and Malaysia.

### Indonesian Diaspora + Foreign Spouses
Cross-border PA cases involving Indonesia frequently arise where one parent is non-Indonesian (Australian, Dutch, American, etc.). The Indonesian framework typically favors the Indonesian-resident parent in practice.

### Recommended Strategy for Cross-Border PA
- Engage Indonesian family-law counsel EARLY
- Document the parties' habitual residence carefully
- Consider preventive measures (passport controls, ne exeat clauses in originating jurisdictions) BEFORE Indonesia becomes the situs
- Mediation through accredited mediators (often more effective than litigation)

## ASEAN + Regional Context

Indonesia is a founding member of ASEAN. ASEAN does NOT have a regional family-law framework comparable to Brussels IIb or EU family-law instruments. Regional cooperation is limited.

ASEAN Human Rights Declaration (2012) Article 19 (right to family life) is the closest regional analog to ECHR Art. 8, but it lacks enforcement mechanisms.

## Practical Application

### Motion Language (Indonesian)
> "Tergugat secara sistematis menghalangi hak akses Penggugat terhadap anak dengan melanggar Pasal 41 UU Perkawinan dan Pasal 14 ayat (2) UU Perlindungan Anak. Penggugat memohon perubahan penetapan hak asuh anak dan/atau penetapan jadwal akses yang dapat ditegakkan secara efektif."

## Cross-Border

- NOT a Hague 1980 signatory — major complication
- Bilateral framework + ASEAN cooperation (limited)
- Significant cross-border practice with Australia (large Indo-Australian families), Singapore, Malaysia, Netherlands (historical colonial ties), USA
- Indonesian diaspora cases concentrated in Saudi Arabia (~1.5M overseas workers), Malaysia, Singapore, Hong Kong, Taiwan, Australia, Netherlands, USA
