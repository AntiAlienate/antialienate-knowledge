# Alienating Tactics ARE Psychological Child Abuse — Regardless of Whether "PAS" Is a Diagnosis: A Primary-Source Evidence Base

> **Purpose.** A citation-led evidence base for the **bridge argument** that the *specific tactics* used in parental alienation — triangulation, parentification, enmeshment, manipulation/gaslighting/coercive control, spousification, coaching, erasing the other parent, false-narrative indoctrination, weaponising the legal system, and withholding contact — are *independently* documented as harmful in the well-established psychological-maltreatment literature, and that each maps cleanly onto existing **DSM-5 V995.51 Child Psychological Abuse** and **ICD-11 QE52.0 Caregiver–Child Relationship Problem** criteria. Prepared for the AntiAlienate knowledge repository (CC BY 4.0).
>
> **The argument this page builds.** The public debate has been stuck on whether *"Parental Alienation Syndrome"* is itself a clinical diagnosis. The WHO, the APA's DSM-5 review committee, the APSAC, GREVIO, and many courts have answered that question in the negative — **PAS is not publicly confirmed as a discrete clinical diagnosis**. But that answer does *not* dispose of the underlying conduct claim. The tactics themselves are documented as harmful to children under decades-old maltreatment, attachment, family-systems, and coercive-control research that long predates the PAS debate. The defensible legal-clinical claim is therefore **not** *"the child has PAS"*; it is *"parent X engaged in [specific documented tactic], which is recognised as psychologically harmful to children under [foundational source], and meets the criteria for DSM-5 V995.51 / ICD-11 QE52.0."*
>
> **Compilation date.** 2026-05-25. URLs verified at compilation. Where the primary published text was not directly accessible at compilation, the citation is marked **\[secondary verification only]** and should be re-checked against the original before public reliance.

## 1. TRIANGULATION

**Clinical definition.** A relational dynamic in which a third party — most commonly a child — is drawn into a dyadic conflict (typically the parental marital dyad) in order to relieve tension between the two original parties. The child carries the affect that the dyad cannot metabolise.

### 1.1 Foundational source: Bowen (1976, 1978)

**Citation.** Bowen, M. (1978). *Family Therapy in Clinical Practice*. New York / Northvale, NJ: Jason Aronson. ISBN 978-0876683347. Publisher record: <https://murraybowenarchives.org/books/family-therapy-in-clinical-practice/> — Amazon record: <https://www.amazon.com/Family-Therapy-Clinical-Practice-Murray/dp/1568210116>

**Bowen's core proposition (paraphrase of his canonical formulation, well documented in the Bowen Center literature).** Bowen describes the *triangle* as "the smallest stable relationship system" — a two-person relationship is inherently unstable under stress and will pull in a third to reduce anxiety. The triangle "contains and dilutes" the anxiety that the dyad cannot tolerate. When a parental dyad is in chronic conflict, the triangulated child becomes the *anxiety carrier* for the system. Bowen identifies this as a key driver of symptom formation in the most-triangulated child of a family. **\[secondary verification only]** for direct verbatim line-quoting — the constructs above are universally attributed to Bowen across the family-systems literature; direct reverification against the 1978 Aronson edition is recommended before public quotation.

**Why load-bearing.** Bowen is the *foundational* primary-source citation. Every subsequent triangulation paper (Buehler, Minuchin's structural extension, Kerr & Bowen 1988, Titelman 2008, Brown 1999) treats Bowen as the originating source.

### 1.2 Modern empirical: Buehler & Welsh (2009) — *Journal of Family Psychology*

**Citation.** Buehler, C., & Welsh, D. P. (2009). A process model of adolescents' triangulation into parents' marital conflict: The role of emotional reactivity. *Journal of Family Psychology*, 23(2), 167–180. DOI: 10.1037/a0014976. PubMed: <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19364211/> — PMC open-access: <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2791501/>

**Verbatim (abstract, per the PMC open-access manuscript).** "This study examined adolescents' emotional reactivity to parents' marital conflict as a mediator of the association between triangulation and adolescents' internalizing problems in a sample of 416 two-parent families. Four waves of annual, multiple-informant data were analyzed. Using structural equation modeling, triangulation was associated with increases in adolescents' internalizing problems, controlling for marital hostility and adolescent externalizing problems. … Adolescents' emotional reactivity to marital conflict mediated the association between triangulation and youth internalizing problems."

**Quantitative outcome.** Triangulation predicted *increases* in internalising symptoms (anxiety, depression, somatic complaints) over a four-year longitudinal window, independent of overt marital hostility. The mediating mechanism is the *adolescent's emotional reactivity* to the conflict they are drawn into.

### 1.3 How triangulation shows up in PA cases

- The alienating parent shares adult marital grievances with the child ("your father lied to the judge").
- The child is asked to deliver messages ("tell your mother she's late again").
- The child is positioned as judge ("you decide if you want to see him").
- The child is recruited as the parent's emotional witness or ally.

Each of these is a textbook Bowenian/Buehler triangulation operation and is recognised as harmful *without any reference to PAS*.

### 1.4 DSM-5 / ICD-11 mapping

Sustained triangulation that produces measurable internalising symptoms in the child meets the DSM-5 V995.51 threshold of "non-accidental verbal or symbolic acts by a child's parent or caregiver that result, or have reasonable potential to result, in significant psychological harm to the child" — and ICD-11 QE52.0 "substantial and sustained dissatisfaction within a caregiver–child relationship … associated with significant disturbance in functioning."

## 2. PARENTIFICATION

**Clinical definition.** A role-reversal in which a child is required to assume the functional and/or emotional responsibilities of the parent — either *instrumental* (cooking, sibling care, household management) or *emotional* (acting as confidant, therapist, or emotional support to the parent). The emotional form is independently documented as the more harmful of the two.

### 2.1 Foundational source: Boszormenyi-Nagy & Spark (1973)

**Citation.** Boszormenyi-Nagy, I., & Spark, G. M. (1973). *Invisible Loyalties: Reciprocity in Intergenerational Family Therapy*. Hagerstown, MD: Harper & Row (Medical Department). Later reissues by Brunner/Mazel and Routledge. APA record: <https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1974-01637-000> — Internet Archive copy: <https://archive.org/details/invisibleloyalti0000bosz>

**Core construct.** Boszormenyi-Nagy and Spark introduce **parentification** as one expression of *destructive parental entitlement* — an intergenerational "ledger imbalance" in which the parent extracts from the child what the parent's own parents failed to give. The child is bound to the parent by "invisible loyalties" that override the child's own developmental needs.

**Why load-bearing.** *Invisible Loyalties* is the standard origin-citation for parentification in the clinical literature — universally cited by Chase (1999), Hooper (2007, 2008), Earley & Cushway (2002), Jurkovic (1997), and the entire downstream programme.

### 2.2 Standard reference: Chase (Ed.) (1999) — *Burdened Children*

**Citation.** Chase, N. D. (Ed.). (1999). *Burdened Children: Theory, Research, and Treatment of Parentification*. Thousand Oaks, CA: Sage Publications. ISBN 978-0761907633. Publisher record: <https://us.sagepub.com/en-us/nam/burdened-children/book7180> — ERIC record: <https://eric.ed.gov/?id=ED441561>

**Chase's definition (per the SAGE volume).** Parentified children are those "compelled to fulfil the role of parent at the expense of their own developmentally appropriate needs and pursuits."

**Two types (as established in this volume and the downstream Jurkovic/Hooper literature).**
- **Instrumental parentification** — the child performs household / financial / caretaking tasks that should belong to the adult (cooking, sibling care, paying bills, translating for non-English-speaking parents). Some instrumental parentification is normative; chronic/excessive forms are harmful.
- **Emotional (expressive) parentification** — the child meets the *emotional* needs of the parent: confidant, therapist, partner-substitute, sounding board for adult problems. **Emotional parentification is consistently identified across the literature as the more damaging form** because it operates covertly and is rewarded by the parent as "closeness" or "maturity."

### 2.3 Modern empirical: Hooper (2007, 2008)

**Citation.** Hooper, L. M. (2007). The application of attachment theory and family systems theory to the phenomena of parentification. *The Family Journal: Counseling and Therapy for Couples and Families*, 15(3), 217–223. DOI: 10.1177/1066480707301290. SAGE record: <https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1066480707301290>

**Citation.** Hooper, L. M. (2008). Defining and understanding parentification: Implications for all counselors. *Alabama Counseling Association Journal*, 34, 34–43. ERIC full-text: <https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ875392.pdf>

**Hooper's framework.** Attachment theory clarifies the *dyadic process* by which parentification operates (the child's internal working model is shaped by being made responsible for the parent's regulation); family systems theory clarifies the *systemic context* (the family structure that compels the role reversal). Hooper (2008) is careful to argue *against* over-pathologising — the article explicitly cautions counsellors to distinguish culturally normative instrumental contributions from harmful chronic role-reversal.

### 2.4 Adult outcomes (well-established across the literature)

The downstream literature (Jurkovic 1997; Earley & Cushway 2002; Hooper 2007, 2008; Schier et al. 2015; Engelhardt 2012; Borchet et al. 2020) consistently associates childhood parentification — especially the *emotional* form — with adult:
- depression and anxiety disorders;
- codependency and difficulty asserting one's own needs in adult relationships;
- insecure / anxious attachment patterns;
- somatisation and chronic stress sequelae;
- elevated risk of intergenerational transmission (parentified children parentify their own children).

Specific effect sizes per construct require direct article-level reverification and are **\[secondary verification only]** at this compilation.

### 2.5 How parentification shows up in PA cases

- The alienating parent confides marital and legal grievances in the child ("I don't know how we'll pay the lawyers because of him").
- The child is positioned as the parent's emotional caretaker ("you're the only one who understands me").
- The child is recruited as protector against the other parent ("you'll take care of me, won't you?").
- The child manages the parent's affect (becomes distressed when the parent is distressed, regulates rather than is regulated).

These are textbook emotional parentification under Boszormenyi-Nagy / Chase / Hooper, with no reliance on PAS as a construct.

### 2.6 DSM-5 / ICD-11 mapping

Chronic emotional parentification — particularly when the child shows internalising symptoms, somatisation, or attachment disruption — satisfies V995.51 (non-accidental verbal/symbolic acts producing significant psychological harm) and QE52.0 (sustained relational dysfunction with disturbance in functioning).

## 3. ENMESHMENT

**Clinical definition.** A pattern of family functioning in which interpersonal boundaries are diffuse to the point that family members' individual identities, emotional states, and decisions are fused. The child cannot develop differentiated selfhood because differentiation is experienced (and treated) as betrayal.

### 3.1 Foundational source: Minuchin (1974) — *Families and Family Therapy*

**Citation.** Minuchin, S. (1974). *Families and Family Therapy*. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press. ISBN 978-0674292369. Google Books record: <https://books.google.com/books/about/Families_and_Family_Therapy.html?id=3lRdLKNTEYcC>

**Minuchin's framework.** Minuchin defines a continuum of boundary clarity in families:
- **Diffuse boundaries → enmeshed subsystems.** Members are over-involved in one another's lives; individual autonomy is sacrificed for a "sense of belonging." Each member's affective state is dependent on the others'.
- **Rigid boundaries → disengaged subsystems.** Members are isolated; communication and affective exchange are blocked.
- **Clear boundaries → functional subsystems.** Both belonging and autonomy are supported.

**Verbatim Minuchin (canonical formulation, paraphrased in the standard secondary literature; direct page-citation against the 1974 Harvard edition recommended).** "The clarity of boundaries within a family is a useful parameter for the evaluation of family functioning." Minuchin treats enmeshment as a *structural pathology* that is causally implicated in child symptom formation, particularly in psychosomatic families (Minuchin, Rosman & Baker, 1978, *Psychosomatic Families*, Harvard UP). **\[secondary verification only]** for exact quoted page references.

### 3.2 Related Bowen construct: Fusion

Bowen's parallel concept of **fusion / lack of differentiation of self** describes the same phenomenon in his framework: the inability of family members to maintain a distinct emotional and intellectual self under family pressure. High-fusion families produce low-differentiation offspring. Bowen Center: <https://www.thebowencenter.org/differentiation-of-self>

### 3.3 Empirical outcomes

The downstream literature on enmeshment in adolescence consistently reports:
- poor *differentiation of self* (Skowron & Friedlander 1998 DSI scale; Skowron 2000);
- identity diffusion / arrested individuation;
- elevated internalising symptoms;
- difficulty forming differentiated adult attachment relationships.

Specific effect sizes require article-level reverification: **\[secondary verification only]**.

### 3.4 How enmeshment shows up in PA cases

- The alienating parent positions the child as their emotional twin ("us against the world").
- The child's preferences are presumed to mirror the parent's (and are policed when they diverge).
- Separation for parenting time is experienced and described as abandonment.
- The child cannot identify their own opinions about the other parent as distinct from the alienating parent's.

This pattern is recognised as harmful under Minuchin and Bowen *without any reference to PAS*.

### 3.5 DSM-5 / ICD-11 mapping

Severe enmeshment that produces measurable individuation impairment and internalising symptoms maps to V995.51 ("non-accidental verbal or symbolic acts … significant psychological harm") and squarely fits QE52.0 as a "sustained" relational disturbance.

## 4. MANIPULATION / GASLIGHTING / COERCIVE CONTROL APPLIED TO CHILDREN

**Clinical definition.** A pattern of psychological tactics — surveillance, isolation, micro-regulation of behaviour, reality-distortion, intermittent reinforcement, induced dependency — designed to control another person's autonomy. When applied to children, the tactics are the same; the asymmetry of power is greater; the developmental damage is greater.

### 4.1 Foundational source: Stark (2007) — *Coercive Control*

**Citation.** Stark, E. (2007). *Coercive Control: How Men Entrap Women in Personal Life*. New York: Oxford University Press. ISBN 978-0195154276 (hb) / 978-0195384048 (pb). OUP record: <https://global.oup.com/academic/product/coercive-control-9780195384048>

**Stark's reframing.** Stark's foundational move is to recharacterise "domestic violence" away from the discrete-incident / physical-injury model and toward a *patterned strategy of control*. Coercive control "is neither primarily domestic nor necessarily violent, but a pattern of controlling behaviours more akin to terrorism and hostage-taking." Stark catalogues tactics including isolation, surveillance, micro-regulation ("beeper games," food logs, control of dress / speech / sexual activity / work) and intimidation. The same tactical inventory is applied to children — directly and via the targeted parent.

### 4.2 Children as direct victims: Katz (2016, 2019, 2020, 2022)

**Citation.** Katz, E. (2022). *Coercive Control in Children's and Mothers' Lives*. New York: Oxford University Press. OUP / publisher record via Edge Hill faculty page: <https://research.edgehill.ac.uk/en/persons/emma-katz>

**Citation.** Katz, E. (2020). When coercive control continues to harm children: Post-separation fathering, stalking and domestic violence. *Child Abuse Review*, 29(4), 310–324. DOI: 10.1002/car.2611. Wiley: <https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/car.2611>

**Katz's central finding.** Children are not merely "witnesses" or "secondary victims" of coercive control in the home — they are *direct targets* of the same tactical inventory: isolation, micro-regulation, induced fear, surveillance, weaponised parenting. Katz's work was the empirical foundation cited by Scottish Women's Aid in the legislative push that produced the *Domestic Abuse (Scotland) Act 2018*, which legally recognises children as direct victims of coercive-controlling conduct (cited at: <https://www.legislation.gov.uk/asp/2018/5>).

**Why critical to the bridge argument.** Katz's framework establishes that **the same tactics that constitute coercive-control abuse of an adult partner also constitute direct abuse of a child when applied to the child.** This is exactly the move the bridge argument requires: the tactic is the abuse, regardless of label.

### 4.3 Gaslighting as a specific tactical subtype

**Popular foundational text.** Stern, R. (2007). *The Gaslight Effect: How to Spot and Survive the Hidden Manipulation Others Use to Control Your Life*. New York: Morgan Road Books / Harmony Books. ISBN 978-0767923613. (Clinical-popular framing; primary-source verification recommended against the 2007 Morgan Road edition: **\[secondary verification only]** for direct quotes.)

**Peer-reviewed foundational text.** Sweet, P. L. (2019). The sociology of gaslighting. *American Sociological Review*, 84(5), 851–875. DOI: 10.1177/0003122419874843. SAGE: <https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0003122419874843>

**Verbatim (Sweet 2019 abstract).** "Gaslighting — a type of psychological abuse aimed at making victims seem or feel 'crazy,' creating a 'surreal' interpersonal environment — has captured public attention in recent years but remains poorly understood. … I argue that gaslighting is primarily a sociological rather than a psychological phenomenon and should be understood as rooted in social inequalities, including gender, and executed in power-laden intimate relationships. … Abusers mobilize gendered stereotypes; structural and institutional inequalities … against victims to manipulate their realities."

**Application to children.** Sweet's analytic move — that gaslighting works by *mobilising the structural authority of the perpetrator* against the victim's reality — applies with greater force to a child, who has *less* counter-evidentiary footing than an adult partner. The alienating parent gaslights the child about: the targeted parent's character ("he doesn't love you"), the child's own memory ("you were scared of him, you just don't remember"), and the child's own feelings ("you don't really want to go").

### 4.4 DSM-5 / ICD-11 mapping

DSM-5 V995.51 explicitly enumerates among the criteria for child psychological abuse: "berating, disparaging, or humiliating the child"; "threatening the child"; "harming/abandoning — or indicating that the alleged offender will harm/abandon — people or things that the child cares about." Each of these maps directly onto the coercive-control / gaslighting tactical inventory above. ICD-11 QE52.0 likewise covers the relational dysfunction.

## 5. SPOUSIFICATION / PSEUDO-MUTUAL ATTACHMENT / EMOTIONAL INCEST

**Clinical definition.** A specific severe form of role-reversal in which the parent treats the child as a partner-substitute — confidant, companion, source of emotional intimacy that should be sought from an adult peer. There is no physical/sexual violation; the violation is of generational and relational boundaries.

### 5.1 Foundational empirical: Sroufe & Ward (1980)

**Citation.** Sroufe, L. A., & Ward, M. J. (1980). Seductive behavior of mothers of toddlers: Occurrence, correlates, and family origins. *Child Development*, 51(4), 1222–1229. APA record: <https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1981-07877-001>

**What the study established (per the standard secondary literature).** Sroufe & Ward operationalised maternal seductive behaviour toward toddlers and examined its correlates and family-of-origin antecedents. The behavioural pattern is associated with greater use of threats and physical punishment and *less* supportive, effective guidance — i.e., maternal seductiveness is part of a broader pattern of inadequate parenting, not benign closeness. The construct was extended in Sroufe et al. (1985) and Sroufe (1993) into the **boundary-dissolution / spousification** literature: Sroufe, L. A., Jacobvitz, D., Mangelsdorf, S., DeAngelo, E., & Ward, M. J. (1985). Generational boundary dissolution between mothers and their preschool children: A relationship systems approach. *Child Development*, 56(2), 317–325. **\[secondary verification only]** for direct verbatim quoting; the construct lineage is universally attributed to Sroufe across the developmental-psychopathology literature.

### 5.2 Clinical text: Love (1990) — *The Emotional Incest Syndrome*

**Citation.** Love, P., with Robinson, J. (1990). *The Emotional Incest Syndrome: What to Do When a Parent's Love Rules Your Life*. New York: Bantam Books. ISBN 978-0553352757. Penguin Random House record: <https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/103844/the-emotional-incest-syndrome-by-dr-patricia-love-with-jo-robinson/>

**Love's definition.** Emotional incest is "a style of parenting in which parents turn to their children, not to their partners, for emotional support." It is the *role-reversal*, not any specific interaction, that produces the harm: "It's not the interaction that warps the child; it is the reversal of roles." Love (a past president of the International Association for Marriage and Family Counseling) describes how the child must suppress their own developmental needs to satisfy the adult's, and is "exposed to experiences well beyond their years." **\[secondary verification only]** for exact page-quoting against the 1990 Bantam edition.

### 5.3 Clinical text: Adams (2011 rev. ed.; orig. 1991) — *Silently Seduced*

**Citation.** Adams, K. M. (2011). *Silently Seduced: When Parents Make Their Children Partners — Understanding Covert Incest*. Deerfield Beach, FL: Health Communications, Inc. (revised and updated edition; originally published 1991). ISBN 978-0757315879. Publisher record (Simon & Schuster reissue): <https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/Silently-Seduced/Kenneth-M-Adams/9780757315879>

**Adams's definition.** "Covert incest occurs when a child becomes the object of a parent's affection, love, passion, and preoccupation. The parent, motivated by the loneliness and emptiness created by a chronically troubled marriage or relationship, makes the child a surrogate partner. The boundary between caring and incestuous love is crossed when the child exists to meet the needs of the parent rather than the parent meeting the needs of the child."

**Adams's diagnostic point** (load-bearing for the PA application). "Identification of this kind of incest is difficult, since covert incest victims often feel idealized and privileged, not violated and abused." The child experiencing emotional incest *experiences themselves as special* — which is precisely why the construct is so directly applicable to the "favourite-parent / preferred-parent" presentation of severe alienation.

### 5.4 Adult outcomes (well-established across the Sroufe/Love/Adams literature)

- identity confusion and role-reversal trauma;
- difficulty forming peer adult intimate relationships (because the "intimate partner" template was set by the cross-generational dyad);
- chronic guilt over normal differentiation;
- elevated risk of intergenerational transmission.

### 5.5 How spousification shows up in PA cases

This is the *characteristic* alienating-parent presentation:
- "best friend" framing of the parent-child relationship;
- the child knows adult financial / legal / sexual / marital details;
- the child fills the role the absent partner would normally fill (companion at events, audience for adult disclosures, regulator of the parent's mood);
- the child is celebrated for being "so mature."

This is *textbook* emotional incest / spousification under Sroufe, Love, and Adams — without any reliance on PAS.

### 5.6 DSM-5 / ICD-11 mapping

V995.51 — "non-accidental verbal or symbolic acts … significant psychological harm." QE52.0 — "substantial and sustained dissatisfaction within a caregiver–child relationship … associated with significant disturbance in functioning." Severe spousification produces both.

## 6. COACHING / SCRIPTING THE CHILD

**Clinical definition.** The active rehearsal or shaping of a child's narrative — what the child says about events, about the other parent, about their own feelings — in advance of or during forensic, therapeutic, or judicial inquiry. The harm is twofold: (a) the false-narrative implantation itself; (b) the suppression of the child's autonomous narrative voice.

### 6.1 Foundational forensic-interviewing literature: Lyon and the Child Interviewing Lab (USC)

**Researcher.** Thomas D. Lyon, Judge Edward J. and Ruey L. Guirado Chair in Law and Psychology, USC Gould School of Law (J.D. Harvard, Ph.D. developmental psychology Stanford). Faculty page: <https://gould.usc.edu/faculty/profile/thomas-d-lyon/> — CV: <https://gould.usc.edu/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/lyon_cv_1-2025.pdf>

**The Lyon research programme.** Lyon's Child Interviewing Lab is the leading U.S. research programme on forensic interviewing of children who have experienced abuse or witnessed violence. The work spans (a) child suggestibility — how leading and repeated questions distort children's reports; (b) the detection of coaching; (c) the design of open-ended, non-leading interviewing protocols (the NICHD Protocol, the "Ten Step" Investigative Interview, and related approaches). USC project announcement: <https://gould.usc.edu/news/usc-launches-nations-first-project-on-child-witness-testimony/>

**Why this is the right citation for the coaching / scripting tactic.** The mainstream forensic-interviewing literature (Lyon; Lamb, Hershkowitz, Orbach & Esplin 2008 *Tell Me What Happened*; Poole & Lamb 1998 *Investigative Interviews of Children*) establishes that **children can be coached to produce false or shaped narratives, and that doing so causes downstream harm** — both forensic (corrupted evidence) and developmental (the child internalises a false reality). Lyon's body of work documents the suggestibility risk and provides the technical basis for detecting it.

### 6.2 PA-specific extensions

**Citation.** Bernet, W., & Baker, A. J. L. (2013). Parental alienation, DSM-5, and ICD-11: Response to critics. *Journal of the American Academy of Psychiatry and the Law*, 41(1), 98–104. Open access: <https://jaapl.org/content/41/1/98.full>

**Note.** The specific "Bernet & Baker (2013) on child coaching in custody cases" cited in pre-research notes — published in *Journal of Forensic Sciences* — is **not publicly confirmed** at this compilation in that exact form. The 2013 Bernet & Baker article in *JAAPL* addresses the DSM-5/ICD-11 question, not coaching per se. The Baker / Bernet research programme has documented alienating-parent strategies (including narrative coaching) across several papers; specific paper-level reverification is needed before public reliance. The foundational and most-defensible citation for the **harm** of coaching remains the Lyon / Lamb forensic-interviewing literature, which is independent of the PA debate entirely.

### 6.3 DSM-5 / ICD-11 mapping

Coaching that produces false-narrative implantation in a child constitutes "non-accidental verbal or symbolic acts" under V995.51 with reasonable potential to cause significant psychological harm — particularly because the child must reconcile the implanted narrative with their own memory, a documented driver of dissociation and reality-testing impairment. QE52.0 also applies where the coaching is a sustained pattern.

## 7. ERASING THE OTHER PARENT

**Clinical definition.** A pattern of conduct in which the alienating parent systematically removes the targeted parent from the child's lived reality — removing photos and possessions, refusing to permit mention, replacing the parent's name with the step-parent's, refusing to acknowledge the parent's existence in the child's narrative history.

### 7.1 Foundational documentation: Baker (2007)

**Citation.** Baker, A. J. L. (2007). *Adult Children of Parental Alienation Syndrome: Breaking the Ties That Bind*. New York: W. W. Norton (Norton Professional Books). ISBN 978-0393705195 (orig.) / 978-1324052968 (current edition). Publisher: <https://wwnorton.com/books/Adult-Children-of-Parental-Alienation-Syndrome>

**Method and finding.** Baker conducted in-depth retrospective interviews with 40 self-identified adult children of alienation. The work catalogues alienating-parent strategies and the long-term consequences. Documented strategies include the *erasure* pattern: removal of photographs and possessions, prohibition on saying the targeted parent's name, replacement of the parent with a step-parent in family rituals and language, and rewriting of family-history narrative. Outcomes catalogued include: low self-esteem, depression, substance use, trust difficulties, divorce, and intergenerational transmission. **\[secondary verification only]** for direct verbatim page-quoting against the Norton edition.

**Honest caveat.** Baker (2007) is a self-selected qualitative sample; it is foundational *descriptive* work on the strategies, not a prevalence study.

### 7.2 Mapping to the broader emotional-abuse literature

The "erasure" pattern is functionally identical to the documented child-maltreatment subtype that **APSAC** identifies in its *Practice Guidelines on the Investigation and Determination of Suspected Psychological Maltreatment* (2017) as **"spurning"** and **"corrupting"** — the deliberate rejection / denigration of a person the child loves, and the systematic destruction of normative attachment. APSAC: <https://apsac.org/guidelines>

### 7.3 DSM-5 / ICD-11 mapping

DSM-5 V995.51 examples enumerated in the manual include "harming/abandoning … people or things that the child cares about." Erasure of the targeted parent — a person the child fundamentally cares about — is a literal-text fit to this V995.51 example. QE52.0 likewise.

## 8. FALSE-NARRATIVE INDOCTRINATION

**Clinical definition.** The sustained inculcation of a false or systematically distorted account of reality — specifically, of the targeted parent's character, history, intentions, and conduct — to the point that the child internalises the narrative and defends it as their own.

### 8.1 Foundational source: Lifton (1961) — *Thought Reform and the Psychology of Totalism*

**Citation.** Lifton, R. J. (1961). *Thought Reform and the Psychology of Totalism: A Study of 'Brainwashing' in China*. New York: W. W. Norton. (Multiple subsequent editions; UNC Press 1989 reissue ISBN 978-0807842539.) Goodreads / publisher records: <https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/549257.Thought_Reform_and_the_Psychology_of_Totalism>

**Lifton's "Eight Criteria" of thought reform** (canonical chapter 22 of the 1961 text). These define the structural conditions under which a totalising environment produces ideological conformity:
1. **Milieu Control** — control of communication within the environment, including the individual's inner communication.
2. **Mystical Manipulation** — orchestrated experiences presented as spontaneous.
3. **Demand for Purity** — the world is split into pure / impure, good / evil.
4. **Cult of Confession** — confession of past "sins" against the system.
5. **Sacred Science** — the doctrine is treated as both ultimate moral truth and ultimate scientific truth.
6. **Loading the Language** — thought-terminating clichés; reduction of complex problems to simplistic insider jargon.
7. **Doctrine over Person** — the individual's lived experience is reinterpreted to fit the doctrine.
8. **Dispensing of Existence** — the system claims the right to decide who has the right to exist.

**Why directly applicable.** The Lifton framework was developed for political/religious systems but has been applied across the manipulation literature wherever a more-powerful party imposes a closed reality on a less-powerful one. The alienating-parent environment satisfies multiple criteria: milieu control (the targeted parent is excluded from the information environment); demand for purity (one parent good / one parent evil); loading the language ("he abandoned us"); doctrine over person (the child's own positive memories are reinterpreted as deception); and dispensing of existence (the targeted parent is "dead to us").

### 8.2 Modern extension: Singer & Lalich (1995) — *Cults in Our Midst*

**Citation.** Singer, M. T., with Lalich, J., foreword by R. J. Lifton (1995, updated 2003). *Cults in Our Midst: The Hidden Menace in Our Everyday Lives*. San Francisco: Jossey-Bass. ISBN 978-0787900519. Publisher record: <https://www.amazon.com/Cults-Our-Midst-Hidden-Everyday/dp/0787900516>

Singer (UC Berkeley clinical psychologist) extends the Lifton framework to mainstream contexts including the family. The book includes treatment of children raised inside high-control environments and recovery considerations.

### 8.3 The Childress AB-PA framework — disclosure

Craig Childress's *attachment-based parental alienation* (AB-PA) framework — proposed in *Foundations* (2015) and on his public blog (<https://drcraigchildressblog.com/>) — explicitly invokes the Lifton-style cultic-coercion model and posits a personality-disordered alienating parent. **AB-PA as a discrete framework is not publicly confirmed as accepted by the APA, APSAC, or mainstream child-maltreatment bodies.** It is included here for completeness; the *Lifton / Singer* foundational sources stand independent of Childress and do not require his framework to operate as the citation for false-narrative indoctrination as a harmful tactic.

### 8.4 DSM-5 / ICD-11 mapping

Sustained false-narrative indoctrination producing the child's internalised rejection of a parent meets V995.51 (non-accidental verbal/symbolic acts producing significant psychological harm) and QE52.0 (sustained relational disturbance with functional impairment).

## 9. WEAPONISING THE LEGAL SYSTEM AGAINST THE CHILD'S OTHER PARENT

**Clinical definition.** A pattern of misuse of family-court procedure — vexatious motions, false emergency filings, ex parte applications, manufactured contempt allegations, serial reopening of settled issues — sustained over time, with the effect (and frequently the intent) of inflicting psychological, financial, and relational harm on the targeted parent and, through them, on the child.

### 9.1 Foundational empirical: Watson & Ancis (2013)

**Citation.** Watson, L. B., & Ancis, J. R. (2013). Power and control in the legal system: From marriage / relationship to divorce and custody. *Violence Against Women*, 19(2), 166–186. DOI: 10.1177/1077801213478027. SAGE: <https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1077801213478027>

**What the study found.** Watson & Ancis documented that the *same* coercive-control tactics used during the relationship are extended through the divorce / custody process: distortion of information, false statements in court, weaponisation of procedural mechanisms, and use of the court itself as an instrument of continued control over the targeted parent. **\[secondary verification only]** for direct verbatim quoting against the SAGE article.

### 9.2 The Legal Abuse Scale: Gutowski & Goodman (2022)

**Citation.** Gutowski, E. R., & Goodman, L. A. (2022). Coercive control in the courtroom: The Legal Abuse Scale (LAS). *Journal of Family Violence*. PMC open access: <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9119570/> — Springer: <https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10896-022-00408-3>

**What the scale operationalises.** Litigation tactics including: filing meritless motions, defying court orders, withholding required disclosure, seeking unjustified adjournments, taking unreasonable negotiation positions — operationalised as a measurable construct so that "legal abuse" can be empirically studied as a form of post-separation coercive control.

### 9.3 Litigation abuse and the child: Bala et al. (2024)

**Citation.** Bala, N., et al. (2024). Exploring litigation abuse in Ontario: An analysis of costs decisions. *Family Court Review*. Wiley: <https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/fcre.12819>

**Definition adopted.** "Litigation abuse" = "a pattern of misuse of the Family Court process that could reasonably be expected to cause distress or harm to the other party or their children that is greater than would occur with the proper use of the family justice process."

### 9.4 How the tactic harms the child

The harm to the child is *direct*, not derivative:
- The targeted parent's resources (time, money, emotional capacity) are systematically depleted, reducing the quality of parenting the child receives during their parenting time.
- The litigation itself displaces parenting (the targeted parent is in court, in mediation, in evaluation appointments — not with the child).
- The child is repeatedly subjected to interviews, evaluations, and proceedings that re-traumatise them.
- The child is positioned as the *stake* of an adult contest, with the alienating parent's narrative ("the court will decide") reinforcing the gaslighting and triangulation tactics already in motion.

### 9.5 DSM-5 / ICD-11 mapping

Sustained litigation abuse fits V995.51 to the extent the conduct constitutes verbal/symbolic acts by a caregiver causing significant psychological harm to the child — typically operating via the conditions and outcomes documented above. QE52.0 applies to the relational dysfunction. The conduct also engages **Article 8 ECHR** (right to respect for private and family life) where it impedes the child's relationship with the targeted parent (see ECtHR jurisprudence in *Sahin v. Germany* [GC] 2003; *Strand Lobben & Others v. Norway* [GC] 2019).

## 10. WITHHOLDING CONTACT AS PUNISHMENT

**Clinical definition.** The deliberate use of denial-of-contact between child and targeted parent — outside of any safeguarding rationale — as a punitive or coercive instrument: typically to punish the targeted parent (for asserting rights, for "non-compliance," for re-partnering, for litigation positions) or to coerce the child (to suppress contact wishes, to align with the alienating parent's narrative).

### 10.1 Foundational source: Bowlby — separation distress

**Citation.** Bowlby, J. (1973). *Attachment and Loss, Volume 2: Separation: Anxiety and Anger*. New York: Basic Books. (Penguin paperback ISBN 978-0140218701.) APA / publisher record: <https://www.basicbooks.com/titles/john-bowlby/attachment-and-loss/9780465005437/>

**Bowlby's central proposition.** Separation from the attachment figure produces a *predictable phasic response* in young children: (1) acute *protest* (distress, search behaviour, calling); (2) *despair* (apathy, withdrawal, decreased exploration); and, if separation is sustained, (3) *detachment* (apparent recovery masking disturbed reattachment, with later signs of insecure attachment and elevated anxiety on subsequent separations). Bowlby treats separation distress as a fundamental driver of psychopathology where the separation is prolonged, repeated, or accompanied by hostile messaging about the absent figure. **\[secondary verification only]** for direct page-quoting against the 1973 Basic Books edition.

### 10.2 Modern extension: post-separation parenting research

The downstream attachment / divorce literature (Lamb 1999; Kelly & Lamb 2000 *Family Law Quarterly*; Warshak 2014 *Psychology, Public Policy, and Law* on overnight care) is unanimous that *frequent, predictable, conflict-free contact with both parents* is protective of child mental-health outcomes, and that *disruption of contact* by one parent — outside of safeguarding rationale — is a documented risk factor for adverse child outcomes. Warshak (2014): "Social science research on parenting plans: Closing the gap between theory, research, and practice." *Psychology, Public Policy, and Law*, 20(1), 46–67. APA: <https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2014-09001-001>

### 10.3 How withholding shows up in PA cases

- The alienating parent cancels parenting time as a "consequence" for the child's or other parent's conduct;
- the alienating parent "cannot make" the child go (despite the parent's legal obligation under court order);
- the alienating parent uses contact decisions as a bargaining chip in litigation;
- the alienating parent enforces "no contact" while telling the child it is the *targeted parent's* fault.

### 10.4 DSM-5 / ICD-11 mapping

DSM-5 V995.51 examples enumerate "confinement of the child" and "isolation of the child" among qualifying acts. Withholding court-ordered contact functions as a form of isolation from a primary attachment figure. QE52.0 applies. **Article 8 ECHR** is engaged.

## The bridge argument: synthesis

The ten tactics catalogued above are not novel constructs invented by the parental-alienation literature. Each is **independently documented**, in primary sources predating the PAS debate by decades, as harmful to children:

| Tactic | Foundational source | Date | Domain |
|---|---|---|---|
| Triangulation | Bowen, *Family Therapy in Clinical Practice* | 1978 | Family systems |
| Parentification | Boszormenyi-Nagy & Spark, *Invisible Loyalties* | 1973 | Family systems |
| Enmeshment | Minuchin, *Families and Family Therapy* | 1974 | Structural family therapy |
| Coercive control / gaslighting | Stark, *Coercive Control* | 2007 | Coercive control |
| Spousification / emotional incest | Sroufe & Ward, *Child Development* | 1980 | Developmental psychopathology |
| Coaching / suggestibility | Lyon / NICHD / Lamb forensic-interviewing programme | 1990s–present | Forensic developmental |
| Erasure | Baker, *Adult Children of PAS* + APSAC "spurning" | 2007 / APSAC | Maltreatment |
| False-narrative indoctrination | Lifton, *Thought Reform* | 1961 | Coercive psychology |
| Litigation abuse | Watson & Ancis (2013); Gutowski & Goodman (2022); Bala et al. (2024) | 2013–2024 | Coercive control / family law |
| Withholding contact | Bowlby, *Attachment and Loss vol. 2* | 1973 | Attachment theory |

The combined evidentiary force of these citations is the load-bearing claim of this page. **None of them require "PAS" to be a clinical diagnosis to operate.** Each tactic is recognised as harmful in its own primary literature, and each maps to existing DSM-5 V995.51 and ICD-11 QE52.0 criteria without requiring the addition of a new diagnostic entity.

### The defensible legal-clinical claim

The argument that survives *Daubert / Frye* admissibility scrutiny in the United States, *Daubert*-equivalent scrutiny in the U.K. and Canada, and the APSAC / Saini et al. (2016) differential-diagnosis standard, is **not**:

> *"The child has Parental Alienation Syndrome."*

It is:

> *"Parent X engaged in [specific documented tactic — e.g., sustained emotional parentification], which is recognised as psychologically harmful to children under [specific foundational citation — e.g., Boszormenyi-Nagy & Spark (1973); Chase (1999); Hooper (2007)], and meets the criteria for **DSM-5 V995.51 Child Psychological Abuse** and **ICD-11 QE52.0 Caregiver–Child Relationship Problem**."*

This is the form the claim must take to be admissible, defensible, and resistant to the gender-asymmetric forensic misuse documented by Meier et al. (2020) and Silberg & Dallam (2019). The tactic-based claim:

1. **Stands on primary sources that predate the PAS controversy by decades** and that no responsible court or evaluator can dismiss as "junk science."
2. **Does not require any contested diagnostic label** — only the documented existence of the tactic and the documented harm.
3. **Maps directly onto codes that DSM-5 and ICD-11 already contain.**
4. **Withstands the APSAC differential-diagnosis test** — because the *tactic* (not a presumption) is what must be proved, and the proof rests on observable behavioural evidence rather than the child's avoidance behaviour alone (which APSAC explicitly cautions is not sufficient evidence of psychological abuse by the other parent).
5. **Cannot be flipped into a counter-allegation** in the way "PAS" is repeatedly flipped in custody litigation, because each tactic-based claim is a discrete empirical proposition about a discrete pattern of caregiver conduct, not a global diagnostic claim about the child.

### What this means for practitioners, parents, and lawyers

- **Stop arguing about whether PAS is a diagnosis.** That argument was lost in the DSM-5 review and is being lost in the WHO ICD-11 framework. It is also being lost in front of skeptical evaluators, courts, and policy bodies (GREVIO, UN Special Rapporteur on VAWG, APSAC).
- **Start arguing the documented-tactic-as-abuse claim.** Identify the specific tactic; cite the specific foundational source; map to V995.51 / QE52.0; document the specific harm to *this* child.
- **Adopt APSAC's epistemic guardrail.** A child's avoidance of a parent is not, on its own, sufficient evidence of psychological abuse by the other parent. The documented *tactic* is what must be shown — independent of the child's stated preferences.
- **Where actual safeguarding concerns are credibly raised, they take precedence.** This is non-negotiable. The bridge argument exists to make the legitimate alienation claim defensible, not to defeat legitimate safeguarding claims. See `/evidence/forensic-operation-in-courts` for the empirical record on PA cross-claim misuse and what to do about it.
