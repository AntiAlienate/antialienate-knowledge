> **Purpose.** A primary-source, citation-led evidence page on what major intergovernmental bodies (WHO, UN, Council of Europe, EU) and global professional associations (APA, APSAC, BPS, PASG, and others) actually say about parental alienation (PA). Prepared for the AntiAlienate knowledge repository (CC BY 4.0).

## 1. World Health Organization (WHO) — ICD-11 and the formal removal of "parental alienation"

### 1.1 Primary source

**WHO Frequently Asked Questions — Parental alienation.** URL: https://www.who.int/standards/classifications/frequently-asked-questions/parental-alienation

### 1.2 The history, verbatim from WHO

The WHO FAQ records a multi-stage history of "parental alienation" within the International Classification of Diseases. Quoted verbatim:

> "During the development of ICD-11, a decision was made not to include the concept and terminology of 'parental alienation' in the classification, because it is not a health care term. The term is rather used in legal contexts, generally in the context of custody disputes in divorce or other partnership dissolution. The broader category of 'caregiver-child relationship problem' was seen as adequately covering aspects of this phenomenon that could be the focus of health services."

> "More recently, proposals to include the terms 'parental alienation' and 'parental estrangement' as index terms for 'caregiver-child relationship problem' were submitted and initially approved. Following online commentary, the WHO-FIC Medical and Scientific Advisory Committee recommended clarification that inclusion of a term for search purposes does not signify endorsement by WHO of the term or its use."

> "Following that clarification, comments and questions have persisted about the misuse of the term to undermine the credibility of one parent alleging abuse as a reason for contact refusal and even to criminalize their behaviour."

### 1.3 The formal removal, verbatim

> "Considering the above, WHO has thoroughly reviewed all materials provided and considers that:
> - Parental alienation is an issue relevant to specific judicial contexts.
> - Inclusion of the term in the ICD-11 will not contribute to health statistics.
> - There are no evidence-based health care interventions specifically for parental alienation.
> - In situations in which an individual labelled with this term presents for health care, other ICD-11 content is sufficient to guide coding.
> - Users may classify cases to 'caregiver-child relationship problem'."

> "Therefore, the index term 'parental alienation' has been removed, as has the parallel index term 'parental estrangement'."

### 1.4 Mandate weight

The ICD is the global statistical classification of diseases used by WHO Member States to record health diagnoses and health-related conditions. Removal of the index term in **February 2020** means PA is not an ICD-11 health condition, index term, or coded entity. The clinical category that remains is **QE52.0 — Caregiver-child relationship problem** in Chapter 24 ("Factors influencing health status or contact with health services"), which is not itself a disease classification but a "factor influencing health status."

### 1.5 What WHO has *not* said

WHO has not opined on whether alienating behaviours can constitute child psychological harm under different framings; its position is confined to ICD-11 classification utility.

## 2. UN Special Rapporteur on Violence against Women and Girls — Reem Alsalem

### 2.1 A/HRC/53/36 — the 2023 thematic report

**Primary citation.** Report of the Special Rapporteur on violence against women and girls, its causes and consequences, Reem Alsalem — *Custody, violence against women and violence against children*, A/HRC/53/36, 13 April 2023, submitted pursuant to Human Rights Council resolution 50/7.
Landing page: https://www.ohchr.org/en/documents/thematic-reports/ahrc5336-custody-violence-against-women-and-violence-against-children
PDF: https://documents.un.org/doc/undoc/gen/g23/070/18/pdf/g2307018.pdf

### 2.2 Definitional finding, verbatim (¶ 9–11)

> "9. There is no commonly accepted clinical or scientific definition of 'parental alienation'. Broadly speaking, parental alienation is understood to refer to deliberate or unintentional acts that cause unwarranted rejection by the child towards one of the parents, usually the father."

> "10. The pseudo-concept of parental alienation was coined by Richard Gardner, a psychologist, who claimed that children alleging sexual abuse during high conflict divorces suffer from 'parental alienation syndrome' caused by mothers who have led their children to believe that they have been abused by their fathers and to raise allegations of abuse against them. He recommended draconian remedies to address the syndrome, including a complete cut-off from the mother in order to 'deprogramme' the child."

> "11. Gardner's theory has been criticized for its lack of empirical basis, for its problematic assertions about sexual abuse and for recasting abuse claims as false tools for alienation, which, in some cases, have dissuaded evaluators and courts from assessing whether abuse has actually occurred. It has been dismissed by medical, psychiatric and psychological associations, and in 2020 it was removed from the International Classification of Diseases by the World Health Organization. Nevertheless, it has gained considerable traction and has been widely used to negate allegations of domestic and sexual abuse within family court systems on a global scale."

### 2.3 Quantitative findings on custody outcomes, verbatim (¶ 19)

> "Parental alienation can have a significant impact on custody outcomes. In the United States of America, data show that rates of custody losses between mothers and fathers differ significantly, depending on which parent alleges alienation. When a father has alleged alienation by the mother, her custody rights have been removed 44 per cent of the time. When the situation was reversed, mothers gained custody from fathers only 28 per cent of the time. Thus, when alienation is accused, mothers were twice as likely to lose custody compared to fathers. This has led to an annual estimate of 58,000 children in the United States being placed in dangerous home environments. In New Zealand, a survey demonstrated that 55 to 62 per cent of mothers reported being accused of parental alienation, often diverting the attention courts from legitimate allegations of abuse."

(The 44 / 28 / 58,000 figures are cited by Alsalem to Joan S. Meier and Sean Dickson, "Mapping gender: Shedding empirical light on family courts' treatment of cases involving abuse and alienation," *Minnesota Journal of Law & Inequality* 35(2) (2017), pp. 311–334.)

### 2.4 Gender pattern, verbatim (¶ 14)

> "The use of parental alienation is highly gendered and frequently used against mothers. A study in Brazil found that women were accused of parental alienation in 66 per cent of cases, as opposed to 17 per cent of cases where a man was accused, and men made more unfounded accusations than women. In Italy, the accusation was also overwhelmingly used against mothers."

### 2.5 Headline conclusion, verbatim (¶ 73)

> "73. The report demonstrates how the discredited and unscientific pseudo-concept of parental alienation is used in family law proceedings by abusers as a tool to continue their abuse and coercion and to undermine and discredit allegations of domestic violence made by mothers who are trying to keep their children safe. It also shows how the standard of the best interest of the child is violated by imposing contact between a child and one or both parents and by prioritizing it, even where there is evidence of domestic violence."

### 2.6 Headline recommendations, verbatim (¶ 74(a)–(l), selected)

> "74. Building on these findings, the Special Rapporteur recommends that:
> (a) States legislate to prohibit the use of parental alienation or related pseudo-concepts in family law cases and the use of so-called experts in parental alienation and related pseudo-concepts;
> (b) States comply with their responsibilities and positive obligations under international human rights law by establishing regular monitoring mechanisms to oversee the effectiveness of family justice systems for victims of domestic abuse;
> (c) States ensure mandatory training of the judiciary and other justice system professionals on gender bias, the dynamics of domestic violence and the relationship between allegations of domestic abuse and of parental alienation and related pseudo-concepts; …
> (j) Training be provided on a mandatory basis for all family justice professionals on the relationship between allegations of parental alienation and domestic violence and sexual abuse; …
> (l) The use of 'reunification camps' for children as part of any outcome in legal proceedings be prohibited; …
> (n) States ensure that independent inquiries are established on the use of the pseudo-concept of parental alienation and its iterations, where appropriate."

## 3. Council of Europe — GREVIO and the Istanbul Convention

### 3.1 GREVIO 3rd General Report on Activities (2022)

**Primary citation.** Council of Europe, *3rd General Report on GREVIO's Activities* (2022).
Landing page: https://www.coe.int/en/web/istanbul-convention/-/3rd-general-report-on-grevio-s-activities (direct PDF returned HTTP 403 at compilation — **[secondary verification only]** for the exact "cross-cutting action" phrasing)

Verbatim text identified across A/HRC/53/36's quotation of the report (¶ 28) and corroborated **[secondary verification only]** through GICJ summaries and the Council of Europe portal:

> "[Ensure] that relevant professionals are informed of the absence of scientific grounds for 'parental alienation syndrome' and the use of the notion of 'parental alienation' in the context of domestic violence against women."

This appears among twelve cross-cutting actions identified by GREVIO in its 3rd General Report on activities.

### 3.2 Istanbul Convention — operative framework

**Primary citation.** Council of Europe Convention on Preventing and Combating Violence against Women and Domestic Violence (Istanbul Convention, CETS No. 210, opened for signature 11 May 2011).

- **Article 31 — Custody, visitation rights and safety.** Verbatim:
> "1. Parties shall take the necessary legislative or other measures to ensure that, in the determination of custody and visitation rights of children, incidents of violence covered by the scope of this Convention are taken into account."
> "2. Parties shall take the necessary legislative or other measures to ensure that the exercise of any visitation or custody rights does not jeopardise the rights and safety of the victim or children."

- **Article 45 — Sanctions and measures.** Requires "effective, proportionate and dissuasive" sanctions for offences established in accordance with the Convention.

Alsalem (A/HRC/53/36, ¶ 28) frames Article 31 and 45 as the binding pegs on which GREVIO's PA criticism hangs:
> "Articles 31 and 45 of the Council of Europe Convention on Preventing and Combating Violence against Women and Domestic Violence require judicial authorities not to issue contact orders without considering incidents of violence against the non-abusive carer and the child and to impose 'effective, proportionate and dissuasive' sanctions. In its monitoring activity to date, the Group of Experts on Action against Violence against Women and Domestic Violence has highlighted the strengths and weaknesses of States parties in the implementation of the two articles with regard to victims of domestic violence, including the widespread use of parental alienation as a means of minimizing evidence of domestic violence."

## 4. European Union institutions

### 4.1 European Parliament resolution of 6 October 2021

**Primary citation.** European Parliament resolution of 6 October 2021 on the impact of intimate partner violence and custody rights on women and children (2019/2166(INI)). OJ C 132, 24.3.2022, pp. 27–44.
URLs: https://www.europarl.europa.eu/doceo/document/TA-9-2021-0406_EN.html and https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:52021IP0406

**Recital U, verbatim:**

> "whereas two of the most prestigious institutions on mental health, the World Health Organization and the American Psychological Association, reject the use of the so-called parental alienation syndrome and similar concepts and terms, since they can be used as a strategy against victims of violence by putting into question the victims' parental skills, dismissing their word and disregarding the violence to which children are exposed; whereas according to the EDVAW Platform recommendation, accusations of parental alienation by abusive fathers against mothers must be considered as a continuation of power and control by state agencies and actors, including those deciding on child custody."

**Operative paragraph 41, verbatim:**

> "condemns the use, assertion and acceptance of non-scientific theories and concepts in custody cases which punish mothers who attempt to report cases of child abuse or gender-based violence by preventing them from obtaining custody or by restricting their parental rights; stresses that so-called parental alienation syndrome and similar concepts and terms, which are generally based on gender stereotypes, can work to the detriment of women victims of intimate partner violence by blaming mothers for their children's 'alienation' from their father, calling into question victims' parental skills, disregarding the children's testimony and the risks of violence to which their children are exposed, and jeopardising the rights and safety of the mother and children; calls on the Member States not to recognise parental alienation syndrome in their judicial practice and law and to discourage or even to prohibit its use in court proceedings, particularly during investigations to determine the existence of violence."

**Honest caveat.** A non-legislative European Parliament resolution is **politically authoritative but not legally binding** on Member States or EU institutions. Its evidentiary value is as a formally adopted political position of a co-legislator.

### 4.2 EU Fundamental Rights Agency (FRA) and European Commission

FRA has published widely on access to justice and on violence against women but has not — to the compiler's knowledge at compilation date — issued a stand-alone Agency Opinion on the parental-alienation construct. **Not publicly confirmed.** The European Commission has not issued a formal Commission Communication confined to PA terminology; the relevant operational instrument is the proposed (and as of January 2026 partially adopted) Directive on combating violence against women and domestic violence, summarized by EPRS briefing: https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/BRIE/2023/739392/EPRS_BRI(2023)739392_EN.pdf

## 5. American Psychological Association (APA)

### 5.1 The 2008 APA Statement — full verbatim text

**Primary citation.** American Psychological Association, *Statement on Parental Alienation Syndrome*, 1 January 2008.
Original URL: https://www.apa.org/news/press/releases/2008/01/pas-syndrome
(At compilation, direct fetch was blocked by Incapsula; archival capture verified via the Internet Archive Wayback Machine: https://web.archive.org/web/2014/https://www.apa.org/news/press/releases/2008/01/pas-syndrome.aspx)

**Full text, verbatim:**

> "January 1, 2008
> Statement on Parental Alienation Syndrome
>
> The American Psychological Association (APA) believes that all mental health practitioners as well as law enforcement officials and the courts must take any reports of domestic violence in divorce and child custody cases seriously. An APA 1996 Presidential Task Force on Violence and the Family noted the lack of data to support so-called 'parental alienation syndrome', and raised concern about the term's use. However, we have no official position on the purported syndrome."

### 5.2 The 1996 APA Presidential Task Force on Violence and the Family

The 1996 APA Presidential Task Force on Violence and the Family produced the report *Violence and the Family* (1996). The Task Force noted, in widely cited language: "there are no data to support the phenomenon called parental alienation syndrome, in which mothers are blamed for interfering with their children's attachment to their fathers." The 1996 Task Force is *not* superseded by a subsequent APA Council of Representatives policy: **the APA's 2008 statement is explicit that the Association "has no official position" on PAS.** **[secondary verification only]** for the exact 1996 Task Force phrasing.

### 5.3 What this means

- The APA has **no formal endorsement** of PA / PAS / PAD as a diagnostic entity.
- The APA's only **official press release on PAS** is the 2008 statement reproduced above.
- The APA does **not** publish a counter-statement labelling PA as pseudoscience; its position is the qualified neutrality of "no official position" combined with the 1996 Task Force's evidentiary concern.
- The DSM-5 (2013) and DSM-5-TR (2022) **do not include** parental alienation, parental alienation syndrome, or parental alienation disorder as diagnoses. The closest adjacent codes are V61.20 / V61.29 "Parent-Child Relational Problem" and V61.21 "Child Affected by Parental Relationship Distress" (CAPRD).
- The APA has not — to the compiler's knowledge — issued a stand-alone Council of Representatives resolution on the intersection of intimate-partner violence and custody framed around PA terminology. **Not publicly confirmed.**

### 6.2 The 2022 Position Statement — load-bearing verbatim passages

**Origin of PAS and the gendered framing, verbatim:**

> "In 1985, the late Richard Gardner, a child psychiatrist, first posited an alternative explanation to a child's disclosure of sexual abuse in the midst of a divorce and a custody dispute: parental alienation syndrome (PAS). Gardner stated the child would collude with the preferred parent, who 90% of the time was the mother, to make allegations against the less preferred parent, who 90% of the time the father, of various transgressions, culminating in an allegation of child sexual abuse. Gardner described PAS as a collaboration between the alienated child and the preferred or alienating parent. In addition, Gardner asserted that the mother might enlist the aid of clinicians, whom he pejoratively entitled validators, in asserting that the child had been sexually abused. Gardner further stated that the vast majority of incest cases were true, but the majority of allegations of sexual abuse in divorce were false (Gardner, 1992). Gardner provided no data to support these opinions."

**APSAC's headline position, verbatim:**

> "APSAC's position is that child safety from abuse and neglect (as defined by law) takes priority over parental right to contact."

**The differential-diagnosis guardrail (the "rule out other explanations" guardrail), verbatim:**

> "It is APSAC's position that professionals who are trying to determine the cause of this resistance, refusal, or fear of contact must conduct a careful evaluation of the child and parents… Professionals should consider multiple explanations for this resistance, refusal, or fear. They should rule out explanations other than parental manipulation before concluding that the child's behavior is caused or is mostly caused by the preferred parent's actions."

APSAC then enumerates the rival hypotheses that must be ruled out — including direct maltreatment by the rejected parent, witnessed abuse, the rejected parent's mental instability or substance use, historical poor relationship, failure to support, disparagement, emotional unavailability, parenting incompetence, lack of developmental knowledge, culture, and gender/race/ethnicity-based child preference.

**APSAC's concluding stance, verbatim:**

> "APSAC stands opposed to the use of PAS, PAD, and PA as a presumptive explanation for child resistance, refusal, and fear of contact with the less preferred parent in contested child custody cases. Science and careful evaluations of the causes of child resistance, refusal, and fear of contact in particular cases should guide investigations by Child Protective Services and evaluations by child custody experts."

**The Saini et al. (2016) empirical weakness finding, verbatim:**

> "The research on PAS, PAD, and PA is weak (Saini et al., 2016). Saini and colleagues found, in a comprehensive review of the research on alienation, that the studies generally used small, non-random samples with no comparison group."

**The reproduced National Council of Juvenile and Family Court Judges warning, verbatim (APSAC pp. 3–4 quoting Bowles et al. 2008, p. 13):**

> "Under relevant evidentiary standards, the court should not accept testimony regarding parental alienation syndrome, or 'PAS.' The theory positing the existence of PAS has been discredited by the scientific community. In Kumho Tire v. Carmichael, 526 U.S. 137 (1999), the Supreme Court ruled that even expert testimony based in the 'soft sciences' must meet the standard set in the Daubert case. Daubert, in which the court re-examined the standard it had earlier articulated in the Frye case, requires application of a multi-factor test, including peer review, publication, testability, rate of error, and general acceptance. PAS does not pass this test. Any testimony that a party to a custody case suffers from the syndrome or 'parental alienation' should therefore be ruled inadmissible and stricken from the evaluation report under both the standard established in Daubert and the earlier Frye standard."

**APSAC's expression of concern regarding recommended separation interventions, verbatim:**

> "Of serious concern is that PA advocates are describing PAB as a form of family violence and recommending children be separated from the preferred parent and placed with the less preferred and allegedly abusive parent (Harman, Bernet, & Harman, 2019)."

### 6.3 The "negligent, even reckless" passage

The phrase **"negligent, even reckless"** as a verbatim load-bearing string does **not appear in the 2022 APSAC Position Statement** as reviewed at compilation. The functionally equivalent text is APSAC's enumeration of evaluator failures and its statement that "PAS, PAD, and PA have continued to be employed in court proceedings related to custody and visitation to support a conclusion that allegations of interpersonal violence are false. PA advocates have used the assertion that these allegations of maltreatment are false to gain leverage in custody disputes. **This is a tragic situation for unprotected children and their protective parents.**" Repositories that quote APSAC as using "negligent, even reckless" should be verified against the primary PDF; the compiler could not confirm that phrase in the 22 January 2022 document.

### 6.4 The "child's avoidance of a parent is not sufficient evidence of psychological abuse" guardrail

The exact string requested is not present verbatim in the 2022 APSAC Position Statement. The functionally equivalent and load-bearing APSAC stance is the differential-diagnosis guardrail in §6.2 above plus the headline conclusion: APSAC will **not** treat a child's resistance/refusal/fear of contact as sufficient evidence of preferred-parent psychological abuse without ruling out the enumerated alternative explanations.

### 7.6 Spain — General Council of the Judiciary and statutory prohibition

Spain's *Ley Orgánica 8/2021, de 4 de junio, de protección integral a la infancia y la adolescencia frente a la violencia* — the Rhodes Law — instructs the judiciary not to rely on PA / PAS as a basis for custody decisions. Alsalem (A/HRC/53/36 ¶ 48) records:

> "To date, there is only one example where the use of parental alienation is explicitly prohibited by legislation, namely in Spain, where the use of these theoretical pseudo-concepts is prohibited as being without a scientific basis and explicitly called out as 'pseudoscience'."

## 10. Synthesis — what the totality of international institutional positions supports

### 10.1 High confidence findings

- **WHO has formally removed "parental alienation" from ICD-11 as an index term (February 2020).** PA is not an ICD-11 health condition, disease, disorder, or index entry. The clinical residual is QE52.0 "Caregiver-child relationship problem," which is a factor influencing health status, not a disease.
- **The APA has no official position endorsing PA / PAS / PAD as a diagnostic entity.** The APA's only official press release (1 January 2008) explicitly states that the APA "has no official position on the purported syndrome," referencing the 1996 Presidential Task Force on Violence and the Family which noted the lack of supporting data.
- **DSM-5 and DSM-5-TR do not list PA, PAS, or PAD.** The proposal of Bernet et al. (2010) for inclusion was not adopted.
- **APSAC (2022) actively cautions against using PA as a presumptive explanation for a child's resistance, refusal, or fear of contact** without first ruling out direct maltreatment, witnessed abuse, and the other enumerated alternative explanations. APSAC opposes the use of PA-driven psychological-abuse findings as a basis for separation interventions.
- **UN OHCHR (Alsalem A/HRC/53/36, 2023; OHCHR press 2025; joint MESECVI–SRVAW communiqué 2022) is sharply critical of the forensic use of PA**, characterising it as a "pseudo-concept" used to negate domestic and sexual abuse allegations and recommending that States legislate to prohibit its use.
- **The European Parliament (resolution of 6 October 2021, ¶ 41) calls on Member States "not to recognise parental alienation syndrome in their judicial practice and law and to discourage or even to prohibit its use in court proceedings".**

### 10.2 Moderate confidence findings

- **GREVIO's position depends on which evaluation report you read.** In its 3rd General Report (2022), GREVIO identified informing professionals of the absence of scientific grounds for PAS as a cross-cutting action. Country-specific reports (Italy 2020, Spain, Portugal, Italy 2024 thematic) reinforce this position with country-specific findings, but the exact wording varies by report. **[secondary verification only]** for the specific cross-cutting-action language; direct rm.coe.int fetches were blocked at compilation.
- **The Italian Supreme Court of Cassation (Sentence No. 9691 of 24 March 2022)** has held that exclusive custody cannot be based only on a PAS diagnosis and that judges must verify the scientific foundation of any deviation from official medical science.
- **The Canadian federal Department of Justice** has published the operational position cited at A/HRC/53/36 ¶ 52 cautioning against PAS labelling.

### 10.3 Mixed picture findings — national psychology associations

- **British Psychological Society (BPS):** active engagement with the Family Justice Council on the regulation and qualifications of expert witnesses in "alienating behaviour" cases; no stand-alone position labelling PA as a clinical diagnosis. The BPS/FJC operational position has shifted in the direction of insisting on HCPC-registered psychologists and on determining domestic-abuse allegations first.
- **Family Justice Council of England & Wales (December 2024 guidance):** widely reported as cautioning against use of PA as a presumptive label in cases with allegations of domestic abuse.
- **Australian Psychological Society:** no stand-alone position on PA as a diagnosis identified at compilation. **Not publicly confirmed.**
- **Canadian Psychological Association:** no stand-alone position on PA as a diagnosis identified at compilation. **Not publicly confirmed.**
- **Royal College of Psychiatrists:** no stand-alone position on PA as a psychiatric disorder identified at compilation. **Not publicly confirmed.**
- **Società Italiana di Psicologia and Italian Ministry of Health:** reported (via Alsalem ¶ 47) to have repudiated PA; **[secondary verification only]** for primary Italian-language sources.

### 10.4 The recognition pole

- **PASG (Parental Alienation Study Group):** sustained advocacy for PA recognition; ~1,000 members across 65+ countries; 2025 *Official Synopsis*; aligned scholars include Bernet, Warshak, Baker. PASG's *organisational standing* is that of a 501(c) advocacy / membership organisation — not an intergovernmental body, not an accredited psychology national society, not a peer-reviewing scientific society. Its institutional weight is consequently asymmetric to that of WHO, APA, APSAC, GREVIO, the European Parliament, or the UN OHCHR.

### 10.5 The synthesis sentence a lawyer or journalist can cite

> Across the major intergovernmental bodies that have spoken on parental alienation — WHO, UN OHCHR, the Council of Europe (via GREVIO), and the European Parliament — and across the major safeguarding professional society in the United States (APSAC), the consistent position is that **parental alienation lacks a validated clinical or scientific basis as a formal diagnostic entity, and its forensic use in custody proceedings poses a risk of negating credible allegations of domestic violence and child abuse**, particularly when used against mothers. **The American Psychological Association has no official position endorsing the construct and has reiterated, since 1996, that there are no data to support PAS.** The principal organised counterweight is the Parental Alienation Study Group, an international advocacy and scholarly membership organisation that argues for recognition; its institutional standing is *not* equivalent to the bodies above.
