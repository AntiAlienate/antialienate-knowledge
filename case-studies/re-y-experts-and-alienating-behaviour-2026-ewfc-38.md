# Re Y (Experts and Alienating Behaviour: The Modern Approach) [2026] EWFC 38

**Court:** Family Court, sitting at the Royal Courts of Justice (Case No: ME25P00554)
**Date:** Judgment handed down 20 February 2026; final hearing 29 January 2026
**Judge:** Sir Andrew McFarlane, President of the Family Division
**Reported at:** [Judiciary.uk PDF](https://www.judiciary.uk/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Re-Y-judgment-final-version.pdf) | [Judiciary case page](https://www.judiciary.uk/judgments/re-y-experts-and-alienating-behaviour-the-modern-approach/)

## Parties

Anonymised. The mother is the applicant on a Part 18 set-aside application; the father is the respondent. Two children: a daughter "X" (age 12 at the 2019 hearing, 18 by 2025) and a son "Y" (age 9 in 2019, 15-16 in 2025-26). The expert at the centre of the case, **Melanie Gill**, is named in the judgment. The trial judge whose findings were set aside was District Judge G Smith (not anonymised in judgment commentary). Parents separated December 2014; proceedings began in 2018.

## Background

After the parents separated, the father issued an application in 2018 seeking the children to live with him. The mother alleged domestic abuse by the father; the father alleged that the mother was alienating the children. A fact-finding hearing on the cross-allegations of abuse was scheduled.

Before fact-finding occurred, in April 2019, the Children's Guardian applied for permission to instruct **Melanie Gill** as a single joint expert "specialist family assessment." Gill described herself as an "assessment psychologist" using "Attachment Science" and the Adult Attachment Interview. She was neither registered with the Health and Care Professions Council (HCPC) nor a chartered psychologist with the British Psychological Society. The Bureau of Investigative Journalism later established that she was a former music promoter and receptionist who had spent 15+ years giving expert evidence on parental alienation.

In September 2019 Gill's report concluded there was "extensive evidence" the mother had alienated the children, that the mother showed "significant psychopathology" from "unresolved trauma," and that the children should be removed to the father. At the October 2019 hearing the judge took Gill's oral evidence first, accepted her analysis, and concluded that no further evidence – including the planned fact-finding on domestic abuse – was necessary. By end of 2019 the children were transferred to the father. The mother had no contact of any kind with them until 2025.

## Key facts

- Children removed from mother's care end of 2019 on the basis of alienation findings made without first determining the disputed allegations of domestic abuse
- Expert Melanie Gill was unregulated: not HCPC-registered, not BPS-chartered, no clinical practice, only seeing families through contested court proceedings
- No formal application or draft letter of instruction existed for Gill's appointment
- Guardian later said Gill's expert view "mirrored her own professional view"
- Mother was made subject to a section 91(14) order restricting further applications
- Mother had no direct or indirect contact with either child from late 2019 to 2025
- Early 2025: daughter X (then 18) briefly moved to mother before returning to father
- November 2025: son Y (then 15) left father's home unilaterally and went to mother; was removed by police at 4am and placed in foster care, eventually accommodated with a family friend
- Late 2025: court granted Y a "live with" order favouring his mother
- April 2025: mother issued a Part 18 application to set aside the 2019 findings, relying in part on the new authority of *Re C ('Parental Alienation': Instruction of Expert)* [2023] EWHC 345 (Fam)

## Procedural posture

A Part 18 application by the mother to set aside historic findings of fact made in 2019-2020, rather than an appeal. The President accepted that Part 18 was the right vehicle because (a) no extension of time was needed, (b) the appeal route was "something of a blunt instrument," and (c) failed appeals carry heightened permission thresholds.

## The judgment

McFarlane P allowed the application. He held that the 2019 findings were "fundamentally flawed and must be set aside" and directed that the core findings be vacated and not re-determined. He identified six foundational errors in the approach:

1. Expert instruction should have awaited completion of fact-finding on domestic abuse
2. An unregulated, unchartered "psychologist" should not have been appointed
3. The Guardian and court failed to scrutinise the expert's qualifications
4. Fact-finding must precede expert opinion evidence in this kind of case
5. Final reports should not have been directed before fact-finding
6. Concluding no further evidence was needed after Gill's testimony was fundamentally wrong

Direct quotes:

> "When held up against the modern approach to cases where there are cross-allegations of domestic abuse and alienating behaviour, together with the approach that should now be taken to the appointment of an expert psychologist in the Family Court, it can be seen that the course adopted in the present case, both as to case management and by the judge in making his core findings of fact, was fundamentally flawed and must be set aside." (McFarlane P)

> "Every agency involved in these proceedings can be seen to have been at fault." (McFarlane P)

For the future, the President held that permission under s.13 Children and Families Act 2014 should not be granted for the instruction of an "expert psychologist" who is neither registered by a relevant statutory body nor chartered by the BPS, save in genuinely exceptional circumstances.

## Why this case matters

Re Y is the most consequential English family-court parental-alienation judgment of the decade. It does three things simultaneously. First, it endorses and operationalises the 2024 Family Justice Council Guidance: alienating behaviours cannot be assessed in a vacuum and domestic-abuse allegations must be determined first. Second, it slams shut the door on the cottage industry of unregulated "alienation experts" who have shaped countless private-law outcomes since the early 2010s – Gill alone is reported to have given evidence in roughly 200 cases. Third, it establishes the Part 18 set-aside route as a viable remedy for historic miscarriages, opening the door to dozens (potentially hundreds) of mothers seeking to reopen findings that severed them from their children.

For practitioners, the operational rule is now clear: no fact-finding, no expert. No HCPC registration or BPS chartership, no permission to instruct. For policy advocates, it is the strongest judicial confirmation yet that the "PA pipeline" – guardian proposes friendly expert, expert finds alienation, court accepts without fact-finding, children removed – was a system-wide failure, not the error of one bad judge. For affected parents, it changes the legal landscape: the door that closed when an appeal route expired may have re-opened.

The case also vindicates – belatedly – a journalism-driven accountability model. Without TBIJ's investigation, Right to Equality's campaigning, and the Transparency Project's reporting, the Gill cases would likely have remained invisible. That synergy of press and litigation is itself one of the substantive facts of the case.
