## Parties

- **Complainant (Beschwerdeführerin):** The mother of two children born 2012 and 2016. She is identified in the public order only by initials, consistent with German constitutional-court practice in family matters.
- **Counsel for the complainant (Bevollmächtigte):** **Rechtsanwältin Kerstin Bielefeld**, Cologne – named on the face of the BVerfG order ([BVerfG PDF cover page](https://www.bundesverfassungsgericht.de/SharedDocs/Downloads/DE/2023/11/rk20231117_1bvr107623.pdf?__blob=publicationFile&v=1)).
- **Indirect Beschwerdegegner:** the father (the other parent in the underlying custody proceedings); not separately represented before the BVerfG, but his interests are reflected in the OLG Köln decisions that the BVerfG reviewed.
- **Subject-matter of the constitutional complaint:** decisions of the **Oberlandesgericht Köln (Higher Regional Court of Cologne)** dated **8 May 2023** (substantive interim transfer of residence-determination right, *Aufenthaltsbestimmungsrecht*) and **17 May 2023** (rejecting the mother's *Anhörungsrüge* / objection that she had not been properly heard), in case **II-25 UF 19/23**.

## Background

The case arose from the breakdown of a German marriage in February 2020 and a long-running, highly conflictual custody and contact dispute over two children, born 2012 and 2016. The proceedings are an emblematic example of the German family-court system's struggle with cases where:

1. one parent (here the mother) is the *de facto* primary carer;
2. contact between the children and the other parent (here the father) has effectively ceased;
3. the resident parent attributes the cessation to the children's own refusal;
4. courts and experts disagree about whether to credit that refusal or to treat it as the product of parental influence – the cluster of phenomena variously described in clinical literature as "Parental Alienation Syndrome" (PAS) or "Eltern-Kind-Entfremdung" (parent-child estrangement); and
5. lower courts then experiment with the most extreme remedy in German family law – transferring the *Aufenthaltsbestimmungsrecht* (the right to determine the child's residence) under § 1671 Abs. 1 S. 2 Nr. 2 BGB, with the result that the children physically change households.

The decision is the most important German constitutional-court statement to date on whether, and how, family courts may use the *Eltern-Kind-Entfremdung* / PAS framework when making such orders. It is also one of a small number of European top-court rulings that frontally engage with the scientific status of PAS.

## Key facts

- The complainant mother and the father separated in February 2020. Two children of the marriage, born **2012 and 2016**, lived primarily with the mother.
- Multiple parallel proceedings over custody, residence, and contact ran in the **Amtsgericht (Familiengericht)** and on appeal to the **Oberlandesgericht Köln**.
- On **17 February 2021** the Amtsgericht awarded the mother the right to determine the children's residence (*Aufenthaltsbestimmungsrecht*) and ordered a near-parity contact regime for the father.
- From **September 2022** the mother ceased to make the children available for contact with the father, on the basis (which she maintained throughout) that the children themselves refused. The family court issued **six successive enforcement measures (Ordnungsmittel)** against her, which did not restore contact.
- The father applied, by way of urgent interim measures, for transfer of the *Aufenthaltsbestimmungsrecht*. By order of **8 May 2023** in case II-25 UF 19/23, the **OLG Köln** transferred that right to the father, with the practical consequence that the children would change households. The OLG dismissed the mother's *Anhörungsrüge* by further order of 17 May 2023.
- The mother lodged a constitutional complaint (Verfassungsbeschwerde) under Article 6 Abs. 2 S. 1 GG (parental rights) and Article 103 Abs. 1 GG (right to be heard).
- On **15 June 2023** the 2. Kammer of the First Senate granted an interim measure (einstweilige Anordnung) suspending the OLG's transfer order pending the substantive decision ([15 June 2023 order](https://www.bundesverfassungsgericht.de/SharedDocs/Entscheidungen/DE/2023/06/rk20230615_1bvr107623.html)).
- On **17 November 2023** the same Kammer issued the substantive Beschluss now under discussion: it allowed the constitutional complaint, set aside the OLG's 8 May 2023 transfer order, remitted the case to the OLG Köln, declared the related 17 May 2023 order moot, and required the State of North Rhine-Westphalia to reimburse the complainant's costs ([BVerfG order, decretal part](https://www.bundesverfassungsgericht.de/SharedDocs/Entscheidungen/DE/2023/11/rk20231117_1bvr107623.html)).

## Procedural posture

A Kammerbeschluss (chamber decision) on a Verfassungsbeschwerde (constitutional complaint) under Art. 93 Abs. 1 Nr. 4a GG and § 90 ff. BVerfGG. Chamber decisions are not Senate judgments and do not formally establish leading constitutional doctrine, but they are binding inter partes and are routinely treated by lower courts as authoritative interpretations of settled Senate jurisprudence on Art. 6 GG. The earlier order of 15 June 2023 was an einstweilige Anordnung under § 32 BVerfGG.

## The judgment

The Kammer held that the OLG Köln's 8 May 2023 decision violated the complainant's fundamental right under **Article 6 Abs. 2 Satz 1 GG** (Elterngrundrecht – the constitutional parental right) and annulled it. The reasoning has two limbs.

### Limb 1 – the constitutional standard for residence-transfer orders

The Kammer reiterated long-standing Senate jurisprudence that, while Art. 6 Abs. 2 S. 1 GG protects the parental right to care for and bring up children, the *child's welfare* (Kindeswohl) is the controlling standard (*maßgebliche Richtschnur*). When a court takes the most invasive step – removing a child from the household of the previously primary carer – it must:

- consider all of the circumstances of the case;
- in particular weigh the *expressed will of the child* (with increasing weight as the child grows older);
- engage substantively with the reports of the *Verfahrensbeistand* (court-appointed child advocate), the *Jugendamt* (youth welfare office) and any court-appointed expert (*Sachverständigengutachten*); and
- where the court departs from those professional recommendations, give reasons that are themselves capable of constitutional review.

### Limb 2 – the rejection of *Eltern-Kind-Entfremdung* / PAS as a sufficient basis

The decisive passage – the one for which the order is now repeatedly cited – is at **Rn. 34** (paragraph 34) of the Beschluss. The Kammer wrote, of the OLG Köln's reliance on alienation as the explanation for the children's refusal of contact:

> "Mit der vom Oberlandesgericht herangezogenen Eltern-Kind-Entfremdung wird auf das überkommene und fachwissenschaftlich als widerlegt geltende Konzept des sogenannten Parental Alienation Syndrom (kurz PAS) zurückgegriffen."

(English translation: "In invoking *Eltern-Kind-Entfremdung* [parent–child alienation], the Higher Regional Court is drawing on the outdated concept – regarded in the specialist scientific literature as refuted – of so-called Parental Alienation Syndrome (PAS).")

The Kammer continued:

> "Soweit ersichtlich besteht nach derzeitigem Stand der Fachwissenschaft kein empirischer Beleg für eine elterliche Manipulation bei kindlicher Ablehnung des anderen Elternteils oder für die Wirksamkeit einer Herausnahme des Kindes …"

(English translation: "So far as can be seen, in the current state of specialist science there is no empirical evidence of parental manipulation underlying a child's rejection of the other parent, nor of the effectiveness of removing the child …")

On that basis the Kammer concluded that an interim transfer of residence-determination right, founded principally on an *Eltern-Kind-Entfremdung* / PAS diagnosis without engaging with the children's own expressed will and the *Verfahrensbeistand*'s contrary recommendation, was not a *kindeswohlorientierte* (child-welfare-oriented) decision and thus did not meet the proportionality requirements of Art. 6 Abs. 2 S. 1 GG.

### Disposition

- 8 May 2023 OLG Köln order: annulled (violation of Art. 6 Abs. 2 S. 1 GG).
- Case remitted to the OLG Köln for fresh decision.
- 17 May 2023 OLG Köln *Anhörungsrüge* order: declared moot.
- Land Nordrhein-Westfalen ordered to reimburse the complainant's necessary expenses.

## Reactions and commentary

- The decision was immediately characterised – correctly – as the strongest German constitutional-court statement to date that *Eltern-Kind-Entfremdung* / PAS cannot, on its own, justify removing children from their primary carer.
- Parental-alienation advocacy organisations and PAS-sympathetic family-law commentators reacted critically. The blog *hochstrittig.org* argued at length that the OLG Köln decision the BVerfG set aside did not actually invoke "PAS" or "Eltern-Kind-Entfremdung" by name, and that the BVerfG therefore attacked a strawman ([hochstrittig.org](https://hochstrittig.org/wie-das-bundesverfassungsgericht-eltern-kind-entfremdung-verurteilt/)).
- Mother- and survivor-side organisations, particularly the *Verband alleinerziehender Mütter und Väter* (VAMV), welcomed the order as a corrective to what they describe as a pattern of OLG-level family senates ordering transfers of residence away from primary carers on PAS-style reasoning ([VAMV Baden-Württemberg case note](https://vamv-bw.de/7399-2/)).
- Within the German family-law commentariat (the leading journals are *FamRZ*, *NZFam*, *ZKJ* and *NJW*), the decision has been read as binding lower courts to (a) treat PAS as scientifically contested, not settled; (b) avoid using *Eltern-Kind-Entfremdung* terminology as if it were a free-standing legal ground; and (c) provide reasoned engagement with the child's expressed will and the *Verfahrensbeistand*'s recommendation whenever a court contemplates an *Aufenthaltsbestimmungsrecht* transfer. Individual published case notes – including in the leading family-law journals – are catalogued at the dejure.org case page ([dejure.org index](https://dejure.org/dienste/vernetzung/rechtsprechung?Text=1+BvR+1076%2F23)).
- The decision sits alongside a small but growing body of European top-court engagement with PAS – including, on the rights-of-the-mother side, the European Court of Human Rights' case-law on Art. 8 ECHR and contact disputes, and, in the academic literature, contributions by **Wilfrid von Boch-Galhau** (Würzburg) for PAS, and by **Ludwig Salgo** (Frankfurt) against. The BVerfG's order is the first time the German constitutional court has effectively sided with the Salgo-style critique.

## Why this case matters

For German family-law practice and for the wider European parental-alienation debate, BVerfG 1 BvR 1076/23 is the moment a constitutional court told a family-law senate, in plain terms, that *Eltern-Kind-Entfremdung* is not a legal shortcut.

Several features make it particularly significant.

First, it is a **constitutional-rights ruling**, not a welfare-of-the-child ruling. The Kammer reasons from **Art. 6 Abs. 2 S. 1 GG**, the parental fundamental right, and the proportionality discipline that comes with it. The Kammer accepts that the child's welfare is the controlling standard, but holds that an extreme intervention – removing the child from the primary carer – has to be supported by reasoning that is itself proportionate and scientifically defensible. PAS-style explanations, in the Kammer's view, do not clear that bar.

Second, the decision is **doctrinally narrower than it reads**. The Kammer does not say that no court may ever consider whether a child's rejection of a parent has been shaped by the other parent. What it says is that the court cannot rely on *Eltern-Kind-Entfremdung* as if it were a settled diagnostic concept, and cannot ignore the child's expressed will and the *Verfahrensbeistand*'s contrary view without giving reasons capable of constitutional review.

Third, it is a useful **calibration** for advocacy. PA-positive commentary frequently treats Strasbourg cases on contact-enforcement (Improta v Italy, Strumia v Italy, etc.) as if they collectively endorsed a PAS framework. BVerfG 1 BvR 1076/23 is a reminder that the same human-rights vocabulary – Art. 6 GG, Art. 8 ECHR, child-welfare, proportionality – can also be deployed *against* PAS-driven decisions when the alternative is the forced removal of a young child from a primary carer.

Fourth, the decision shows the **civil-law structure** of the German family-court system at work. Unlike English or US family courts, German courts operate under a constitutional regime in which parental rights are explicitly anchored in the constitution (Art. 6 GG) and in which the *Sachverständigengutachten* (court-appointed expert report) and the *Verfahrensbeistand* play formalised roles. The BVerfG's intervention is precisely about whether those formal inputs were taken seriously by the OLG – not about applying a free-standing welfare test as a common-law court would.

For anyone building an evidence base on parental alienation, this case has to be read alongside, and in tension with, the PA-positive English and Australian cases (e.g. Re S, Re A, Re Y, Green & Green) and the Strasbourg failure-to-enforce-contact line (Improta and others). It is the single clearest example to date of a top European court treating the PAS framework itself as the problem.
