# T.L.D v B.G (015642/2022) [2023] ZAGPJHC 801 (13 July 2023)

**Court:** High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Local Division, Johannesburg / Gauteng Local Division of the High Court (sitting as upper guardian of all minors in its jurisdiction)
**Date of judgment:** 13 July 2023 (heard 9 May 2023)
**Judge:** **Adams J** ([Law Library entry for parallel citation [2023] ZAGPJHC 821](https://lawlibrary.org.za/akn/za-gp/judgment/zagpjhc/2023/821/eng@2023-07-13))
**Case number:** 015642/2022
**Reported at:** [SAFLII – [2023] ZAGPJHC 801 (13 July 2023)](https://www.saflii.org/za/cases/ZAGPJHC/2023/801.html) | [SAFLII – application for leave to appeal, [2023] ZAGPJHC 872 (4 August 2023)](https://www.saflii.org/za/cases/ZAGPJHC/2023/872.html) | [Law Library parallel citation, D T L v G B [2023] ZAGPJHC 821 (13 July 2023)](https://lawlibrary.org.za/akn/za-gp/judgment/zagpjhc/2023/821/eng@2023-07-13)
**Related interim order:** Wilson AJ, 22 November 2022 (appointing curator ad litem and clinical psychologist) — discussed in the merits judgment

## Parties

- **Applicant ("T.L.D"):** the mother of the minor child, anonymised in the published judgment in accordance with South African practice for matters concerning children.
- **Respondent ("B.G"):** the father of the minor child, also anonymised.
- **Minor child ("M"):** a boy born **22 February 2015**, approximately 8 years old at the date of judgment. The parents never married but had jointly planned the child's conception.
- **Curator ad litem for the minor child:** **Advocate Mark Leonard Haskins SC**, advocate of the High Court of South Africa, appointed by Wilson AJ on 22 November 2022 ([Family Laws South Africa case analysis](https://familylaws.co.za/parental-alienation-syndrome-landmark-case-south-africa/)).
- **Court-appointed clinical psychologist:** **Mr Leonard Carr**, instructed to conduct a full investigation into the child's circumstances; produced his final report by 22 February 2023.
- **Additional expert evidence:** **Dr De Wit**, whose assessment characterised the father's conduct as driven by "pathological envy" and what she described as "spoiling hostility" toward the mother ([Family Laws South Africa case analysis](https://familylaws.co.za/parental-alienation-syndrome-landmark-case-south-africa/)).

## Background

South Africa's framework for matters concerning children is anchored in **section 28(2) of the Constitution** ("a child's best interests are of paramount importance in every matter concerning the child") and in the **Children's Act 38 of 2005**. The Act does not use the term "parental alienation"; its operative concepts are parental responsibilities and rights (sections 18–22), the best-interests standard (section 7), and the participation of the child (section 10), among others. The High Court sits as the upper guardian of all minors in its jurisdiction and has historically exercised wide-ranging powers to suspend, vary or restructure contact in pursuit of the child's welfare.

T.L.D v B.G is among the most extensively reasoned recent South African judgments on parental alienation. It concerns a child born outside marriage whose parents had jointly planned his conception and who had been the subject of nine previous court orders by the time the mother brought the urgent application that produced the 13 July 2023 judgment. The Court was confronted with concurrent expert evidence — from a court-appointed clinical psychologist and from the curator ad litem appointed under the High Court's upper-guardian jurisdiction — that the child was suffering from moderate parental alienation directed at the mother and her family of origin.

## Key facts

- The parties were never married but had jointly agreed to conceive M, who was born **22 February 2015** ([Family Laws South Africa case analysis](https://familylaws.co.za/parental-alienation-syndrome-landmark-case-south-africa/)).
- The relationship between the parents broke down and litigation over parental responsibilities and contact had run continuously since M's birth.
- A shared residency arrangement on a fortnightly alternating basis had been put in place by an order of **Moosa J** dated **5 June 2020**, after the breakdown of earlier arrangements.
- By 2022 there had been **nine previous court orders** addressing parental disputes about M; three different parenting coordinators had been appointed and none retained.
- On **22 November 2022**, **Wilson AJ** appointed **Advocate Mark Leonard Haskins SC** as curator ad litem for M and directed that **Mr Leonard Carr**, a clinical psychologist, undertake a full investigation into M's circumstances.
- In **January 2023**, M was recorded as stating that he would "sacrifice his own life" if he could not live with his father — a statement central to Carr's subsequent assessment of the child's psychological state ([Family Laws South Africa case analysis](https://familylaws.co.za/parental-alienation-syndrome-landmark-case-south-africa/)).
- By **22 February 2023**, Carr had completed his investigation and produced his final report.
- Carr concluded that M was suffering from **moderate parental alienation** with pronounced negative attitudes toward the mother and the maternal grandparents; early features of **shared psychotic disorder ("folie à deux")** in the father–child dynamic; loss of natural empathic response toward the mother when in the father's company; and the development of an "adaptive false self".
- **Dr De Wit** provided concurring expert evidence framing the father's underlying motivation in terms of pathological envy and "spoiling hostility".
- The curator ad litem, Haskins SC, supported Carr's findings and described M's expressed views as a "robotic" recitation of programmed statements against the mother.

## Procedural posture

An urgent application brought by the mother in the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court for restructuring of parental responsibilities, suspension of the father's contact pending therapy, and ancillary orders. The father brought a counter-application seeking further expert reviews. Heard on **9 May 2023**; judgment delivered on **13 July 2023** by **Adams J**.

## The judgment

Adams J granted the substantive relief sought by the mother and refused the father's counter-application.

The Court accepted the expert evidence of Carr, supported by Dr De Wit and the curator ad litem, that M was suffering from **moderate parental alienation syndrome**. The Court found that M's expressed preference to live with the father could not be afforded its ordinary weight under section 10 of the Children's Act, because that preference was the product of the alienating dynamic itself rather than an autonomous expression of the child's views. The Court emphasised, in line with established South African authority on the upper-guardian jurisdiction, that "a court should not hesitate to intervene for the welfare of the minor child" and that the circumstances called for "drastic measures" ([Family Laws South Africa case analysis](https://familylaws.co.za/parental-alienation-syndrome-landmark-case-south-africa/)).

The orders made by Adams J included the following structural elements (as summarised in the published case analyses; for the verbatim operative part, see the SAFLII judgment):

1. The Moosa J order of 5 June 2020 was suspended pending finalisation of the proceedings, save for specified paragraphs.
2. **Full parental responsibilities and rights and primary residence** of M were awarded to the mother.
3. The father was restricted to specific parental responsibilities only.
4. **All contact between M and the father was suspended for a period of three months** to allow therapeutic work to begin.
5. After the three-month period, contact was to resume on a **supervised basis** (two hours twice weekly plus two hours at weekends), facilitated by a qualified social worker nominated through the Gauteng Family Law Forum.
6. M was to continue in **therapy directed at parental alienation, reconstruction and attachment repair** with a psychologist qualified or experienced in the treatment of parental alienation.
7. A controlled reintroduction of the father to M was to follow upon the treating psychologist's confirmation of M's readiness.
8. Cost provisions were made: costs of the child's psychologist were to be shared equally; the father was to bear all costs of contact supervision; the father was ordered to pay the applicant's costs and the costs of the counter-application.
9. The parties were directed to jointly request appointment of a judicial case manager within five days.

The Court rejected the father's counter-application for further expert assessments, holding that Carr's report was "well-reasoned and grounded in sound principles" and that further delays would not serve M's interests ([Family Laws South Africa case analysis](https://familylaws.co.za/parental-alienation-syndrome-landmark-case-south-africa/)).

An application by the father for leave to appeal was subsequently refused by the same Court on **4 August 2023** ([SAFLII – [2023] ZAGPJHC 872](https://www.saflii.org/za/cases/ZAGPJHC/2023/872.html)), leaving the substantive orders intact.

## Reactions and commentary

- **Family Laws South Africa** (a leading South African family-law commentary platform) published a substantial case analysis treating T.L.D v B.G as a landmark judgment on parental alienation in South Africa and tracing the Court's reliance on the Carr and De Wit assessments and the role of the curator ad litem ([Family Laws South Africa](https://familylaws.co.za/parental-alienation-syndrome-landmark-case-south-africa/)).
- The same platform's broader analytical piece situates the judgment within South African family-law doctrine, the Children's Act 38 of 2005 framework, the section 28(2) constitutional best-interests standard, and the High Court's upper-guardian jurisdiction ([Family Laws South Africa – "Parental Alienation in South African Family Law"](https://familylaws.co.za/parental-alienation-south-africa-family-law/)).
- Subsequent South African judgments have cited and built on the T.L.D v B.G framework, including **B.P.M v J.L.M (1909/2024) [2025] ZALMPPHC 96 (13 May 2025)**, in which the Limpopo Division addressed concerns that the respondent's conduct had the "potential to alienate" minor children from their father and engaged with the Office of the Family Advocate's report ([Family Laws South Africa – B.P.M v J.L.M case note](https://familylaws.co.za/rule-43-parental-alienation-divorce-limpopo-2025/)).
- **M.D v L.N and Another (Reasons) (3051/2020) [2025] ZAECQBHC 4 (3 February 2025)** in the Eastern Cape Division applied a similar framework, appointing a child psychologist to investigate alienation and propose therapeutic intervention ([SAFLII – M.D v L.N and Another](https://www.saflii.org/za/cases/ZAECQBHC/2025/4.html)).
- South African family-law practitioner commentary (for example, [Vermeulen Attorneys](https://www.vermeulenlaw.co.za/parental-alienation-syndrome/) and [Faure & Faure](https://faurefaure.co.za/parental-alienation-and-the-rights-of-parents/)) routinely cites the suspension-of-contact-pending-therapy structure pioneered in T.L.D v B.G as the operative South African model for severe alienation cases.

## Why this case matters

For South Africa — a constitutional democracy with one of the most explicitly child-rights-oriented constitutional provisions in the world (section 28) and an integrated children's statute (the Children's Act 38 of 2005) — T.L.D v B.G is the most fully reasoned recent High Court judgment on parental alienation. It demonstrates how the Children's Act framework, without using the words "parental alienation", can be operated through expert evidence, the curator ad litem mechanism, the High Court's upper-guardian jurisdiction and the best-interests standard to produce a tightly structured therapeutic remedy.

Three features of the judgment travel particularly well beyond South Africa.

First, **structural use of independent expert evidence**: the Court did not rely on warring partisan experts. It appointed a clinical psychologist (Carr), with an independent curator ad litem (Haskins SC), and treated their concurrent findings — supported by Dr De Wit — as the evidentiary spine of the case. That is a model many jurisdictions struggle to deliver consistently in alienation cases.

Second, **suspension of contact paired with therapy rather than punitive denial**: the three-month suspension was not framed as punishment of the father but as therapeutic space for M's "reconstruction" work. Supervised contact was to resume in a graduated way upon the treating psychologist's confirmation of readiness. That structure — suspension to enable therapy, not as substitute for it — is increasingly the operative template in serious alienation matters internationally.

Third, **refusal to give dispositive weight to the child's expressed preference where that preference is itself a product of alienating dynamics**: section 10 of the Children's Act requires that the child's voice be heard, but Adams J held that the weight given to M's voice had to take account of the alienating context in which it had been formed. That doctrinal move — common in principle but rarely executed with this much evidentiary support — is exactly what parental-alienation cases routinely require but seldom receive.

For South African families and for African and Commonwealth family-law systems more broadly, T.L.D v B.G is a fully reasoned, well-evidenced, post-Children's Act precedent that demonstrates that severe alienation can be addressed within the existing best-interests framework without statutory amendment. For the parental-alienation movement internationally, it is one of the strongest examples of a court using expert evidence, an independent voice for the child, and a structured therapeutic order to interrupt an alienating dynamic before it became permanent.
