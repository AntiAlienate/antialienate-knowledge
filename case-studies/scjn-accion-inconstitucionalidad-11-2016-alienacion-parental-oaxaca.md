  - Gobernador Constitucional del Estado de Oaxaca (state governor who promulgated)

- **Affected third parties:** All future litigants in Oaxaca family-court proceedings invoking the parental-alienation provisions of the Civil Code as amended.

## Background

On 2 January 2016 Oaxaca published in its state gazette **Decreto 1380**, which amended the Civil Code for the State of Oaxaca to introduce explicit recognition of "alienación parental" (parental alienation). Three provisions were of central importance:

- **Article 336 Bis B** (last paragraph) defined parental alienation as: "*la conducta llevada a cabo por uno de los progenitores [...] que transforme la conciencia de un menor con el objeto de impedir, obstaculizar o destruir sus vínculos con uno de sus progenitores*" — conduct by one parent that **transforms the consciousness** of a minor in order to prevent, obstruct or destroy the child's bond with the other parent. The provision categorised this conduct as a species of *violencia familiar* (family violence).
- **Article 429 Bis A** (first paragraph) provided for **suspension or loss of patria potestad** (parental authority) as a consequence of parental-alienation conduct ("**bajo pena de suspensión o pérdida de su ejercicio**").
- **Article 459 fracción IV** listed parental-alienation conduct as a ground for loss of patria potestad.

Decree 1380 was, at the time, one of the more aggressive state-level PA codifications in Mexico, sitting alongside earlier reforms in the Federal District (now CDMX) civil code §323 Septimus (2014) and similar provisions in Aguascalientes, Baja California, Michoacán and elsewhere.

On 2 February 2016 the **Defensoría de los Derechos Humanos del Pueblo de Oaxaca** filed an *acción de inconstitucionalidad* (Mexico's abstract constitutional-review mechanism under Article 105 of the Constitution) against these provisions. The case was registered as Acción de Inconstitucionalidad **11/2016** and turned to the Pleno of the SCJN as ponente Ministra **Norma Lucía Piña Hernández** (a First Chamber justice known for family-law jurisprudence).

## Key facts

- **2 January 2016** — Oaxaca publishes Decreto 1380 amending its Civil Code.
- **2 February 2016** — DDHPO files Acción de Inconstitucionalidad 11/2016, raising claims under Articles 1, 4 (best-interests-of-the-child principle) and 14 (legal certainty) of the Federal Constitution, and against several international instruments (CEDAW, Convention on the Rights of the Child, Inter-American Convention on the Prevention, Punishment and Eradication of Violence against Women "Belém do Pará").
- DDHPO arguments: (i) "parental alienation" lacks scientific consensus as a clinical category; (ii) the definition is impermissibly vague (violating legal certainty); (iii) automatic loss of *patria potestad* fails the proportionality and best-interests tests; (iv) the provision is gender-discriminatory in operation, disproportionately weaponised against mothers in custody disputes (Argument grounded in feminist legal commentary and CEDAW jurisprudence).
- The case sat with the Pleno for over a year of analysis.
- **23, 24, 26 October 2017** — Pleno discussion and vote. Minister José Ramón Cossío Díaz absent on 24 October 2017 (noted in the engrose).
- **16 May 2018** — Sentencia and concurring vote of Minister Aguilar Morales published in DOF.

## Procedural posture

Acciones de inconstitucionalidad are Mexico's mechanism of abstract review under Article 105 fracción II of the Constitution. Standing belongs (among others) to state human-rights ombudsmen in respect of legislation enacted at the state level — the DDHPO had standing to challenge Oaxaca's amendments.

The Pleno sits *en banc* with 11 ministros; declarations of unconstitutionality of legislative acts require a qualified majority of **8 votes**. Individual articles can be invalidated on individual votes; not every Article challenged in this action met the 8-vote threshold.

## The judgment

The SCJN's Pleno reached a **partial holding**: it **invalidated** specified portions of the challenged articles while **upholding** the basic legislative recognition of "parental alienation" as a relevant family-law phenomenon. Specifically:

**Invalidated by qualified majority:**
- **Article 336 Bis B, last paragraph** — the portion defining parental alienation in terms of "transformar la conciencia" (transforming the consciousness) of a minor. The Pleno held this definition was **impermissibly vague** and required proof of an essentially metaphysical state ("transformation of consciousness") rather than describing concrete obstructive conduct. As the Court reasoned, conditioning family-violence classification on so extreme a psychological harm in fact **tolerates lesser alienating conduct** rather than preventing it, breaking with conventional and constitutional standards binding on the Mexican State.
- **Article 429 Bis A, first paragraph**, in its normative portion "*bajo pena de suspensión o pérdida de su ejercicio*" — the automatic-sanction language tying parental-alienation conduct to suspension or loss of *patria potestad*.
- **Article 459, fracción IV** — listing parental alienation as a ground for loss of *patria potestad*.

**Upheld:**
- The duty in **Article 429 Bis A** (in its non-invalidated portion) requiring the custodial parent to "**promover el respeto y contacto constante entre los menores y el otro progenitor**" (promote constant respect and contact between the children and the other parent) and to refrain from acts of parental alienation aimed at producing **rechazo, resentimiento, odio, miedo o desprecio** (rejection, resentment, hatred, fear or contempt) toward the other parent. The Pleno held this affirmative duty was constitutionally permissible — Mexican family law may impose on a custodial parent the obligation of safeguarding the child's relationship with the non-custodial parent.

**Core constitutional reasoning** (as captured in the Crónica oficial and in El Universal's contemporaneous reporting):

- Loss of *patria potestad* "**must not be applied automatically**, but should be a last resort, the judge having to deploy various prior measures, particularly in cases involving family violence." (Crónica, SCJN.)
- The Court accepted, on the basis of expert testimony in the file, that "**unjustified rejection behaviours by children toward their parents in the context of family conflicts and custody disputes**" do occur and may legitimately be regulated by family law (position of ponente Piña Hernández).
- However, the Pleno also accepted (over partial disagreement) that **there is no scientific consensus on parental alienation as a clinical syndrome**, and accordingly definitions that depend on extreme psychological harm or that mandate automatic sanctions cannot stand the test of legal certainty.

Minister **Arturo Zaldívar Lelo de Larrea** intervened on the term "*conciencia*" (consciousness) — questioning what it could mean operationally: "*¿qué significa "transformar la conciencia"? Esto podría aplicarse a cualquier disputa de custodia*" ("what does 'transform consciousness' mean? This could apply to any custody dispute") — per Cimacnoticias reporting.

Minister **Margarita Luna Ramos** defended the broader codification: "**Las legislaciones deben reconocer que estas conductas existen y requieren regulación**" ("Legislatures must recognise that these behaviours exist and require regulation"), notwithstanding the lack of scientific consensus.

**Concurring vote (voto concurrente):** Ministro **Luis María Aguilar Morales** filed a published concurring opinion, included in the DOF text of 16 May 2018.

## Why this case matters

This is the SCJN's leading abstract-review decision on parental alienation and remains binding precedent for every state in Mexico considering or already operating PA-codification statutes. Its doctrinal contribution is precise and exportable:

1. **PA recognition is constitutionally permissible** at state legislative level — the SCJN refused to declare the underlying concept invalid even though it accepted that no scientific consensus exists on PA as a syndrome. This makes Mexico one of the clearest cases globally of an apex court that has analysed PA on the merits and refused to dismiss it as junk science, while remaining critical.

2. **Automatic loss of patria potestad is unconstitutional** as a sanction for PA conduct. The Court anchored this in the best-interests-of-the-child principle (Article 4 of the Federal Constitution and CRC Article 3) and in proportionality. Loss of custody must be a last resort, after escalating intermediate remedies.

3. **The custodial parent has an affirmative duty** to promote constant respect and contact with the other parent (upheld portion of Article 429 Bis A) — i.e. PA-style obstructive behaviour is recognised as a family-law wrong even if it can't trigger automatic sanction. This affirmative duty is unusually direct, and gives litigants a constitutional hook for arguing that family courts must take active enforcement steps when contact is being obstructed.

4. **Definitions matter.** The Court struck down the "transform consciousness" formulation as too extreme and impermissibly vague. This is a useful cautionary precedent for any jurisdiction drafting PA statutes — the SCJN essentially says: regulate the conduct concretely, don't regulate the alleged mental effect on the child.

5. **The case crystallised a four-way Mexican stakeholder split** — feminist organisations (against any PA recognition, citing weaponisation against DV-survivor mothers), parental-equality advocates (for stronger sanctions), child-rights organisations (split, mostly for proportionality), and family-court judges (mostly relieved at having discretion preserved). This split has continued to shape Mexican family-law reform through 2025 (see Tlaxcala AI 74/2024).

For the AntiAlienate knowledge base, this is the **anchor Mexican / Latin American Spanish-civil-law case** — sitting alongside the Brazilian STJ trilogy (Andrighi 2024, GO 2024, Polish/Italian/Spanish counterparts) and the Spanish TS 519/2017 in the global Spanish-language PA jurisprudence corpus. It is the single most-cited Mexican authority on parental alienation and is essential reading for anyone working in Latin American family-law PA litigation.

It also remains good law as of May 2026: the 2025 Tlaxcala decision (AI 74/2024) explicitly re-applied the 11/2016 framework, indicating the SCJN's continued commitment to the proportionality-not-prohibition doctrine.
