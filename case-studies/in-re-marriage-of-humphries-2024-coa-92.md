## Background

The Humphries marriage was dissolved by a Colorado district court in 2012 on terms of **equal parenting time and joint decision-making**. In 2019 the district court modified those orders to allocate **sole decision-making** to the mother. In 2020 the father filed four separate motions alleging that the mother had violated the parenting-time provisions of the May 2019 order ([Colorado Lawyer summary](https://cl.cobar.org/from-the-courts/in-re-marriage-of-humphries/); [Justia summary](https://law.justia.com/cases/colorado/court-of-appeals/2024/22ca1327.html)).

The district court held a **five-day evidentiary hearing in July 2022** on the father's motions. It made findings on three distinct sets of issues: (a) the mother's allegations that the father had abused the children; (b) the father's allegations that the mother had alienated the children from him; and (c) the mother's compliance with the May 2019 parenting-time order.

The district court found the mother's child-abuse allegations against the father **unsupported**, found that the mother had **"severely alienated"** the children from the father, and found that the mother had violated the May 2019 order ([Justia summary](https://law.justia.com/cases/colorado/court-of-appeals/2024/22ca1327.html); [Colorado Lawyer summary](https://cl.cobar.org/from-the-courts/in-re-marriage-of-humphries/)).

As remedies the district court (i) reallocated **sole decision-making responsibility** to the father, (ii) imposed a **$36,500 civil fine** on the mother, and (iii) awarded the father his **attorney fees, costs and expenses**. The mother appealed.

## Key facts

The trial court's findings of alienating behaviour, as summarised in the appellate opinion and the Colorado Lawyer note, included the following:

- The mother **denigrated** the father in emails and text messages to or about him, calling him "idiot, stupid, and a jerk" ([Colorado Lawyer summary](https://cl.cobar.org/from-the-courts/in-re-marriage-of-humphries/)).
- She required the children to **memorise her telephone number** so they could call her "the next time daddy is mean" ([Colorado Lawyer](https://cl.cobar.org/from-the-courts/in-re-marriage-of-humphries/)).
- She encouraged the children to **keep secrets** from their father and to **plot to run away** from him.
- She **interfered with the father's parenting time** by constant phone contact with the children and by watching the children's activities during the father's time.
- She **encouraged the children to report** on the father's parenting style and activities to her.
- She urged on a **campaign of denigration**, including "incessant complaints" about the father to the children ([Colorado Lawyer summary](https://cl.cobar.org/from-the-courts/in-re-marriage-of-humphries/)).

The trial court found these conclusions on a "deep well" of evidence including expert and lay witness testimony and documents accumulated over many years ([Colorado Lawyer](https://cl.cobar.org/from-the-courts/in-re-marriage-of-humphries/)).

## Procedural posture

An appeal under Colorado law from a post-dissolution order entered by the district court following a contested five-day evidentiary hearing. The appellate court reviewed (i) the statutory framework for parenting-time disputes under Colorado Revised Statutes §§ 14-10-129.5 and 14-10-131; (ii) the admissibility of expert testimony on parental alienation; (iii) the legitimacy of the trial court's specific remedies (decision-making modification and civil fine); and (iv) attorney-fee awards.

## The decision

The Colorado Court of Appeals issued a **mixed disposition**: *"the appeal was dismissed in part, reversed in part, and affirmed in part, and the case was remanded for further proceedings"* ([Colorado Lawyer summary](https://cl.cobar.org/from-the-courts/in-re-marriage-of-humphries/)).

### What the Court of Appeals *affirmed* about the alienation finding

This is the part of the opinion that distinguishes *Humphries* from a "PA finding reversed" case. The Court of Appeals **affirmed the trial court's "severe alienation" finding** and the admissibility of the underlying expert testimony.

Specifically:

- The Court of Appeals **rejected the mother's pre-hearing motion to exclude** the expert testimony on parental alienation, finding the trial court had made sufficient findings on the reliability of the evidence and that "*the theory was reasonably reliable under the totality of circumstances*" ([Colorado Lawyer summary](https://cl.cobar.org/from-the-courts/in-re-marriage-of-humphries/)).
- The Court of Appeals **rejected the mother's argument** "*that there can be no parental alienation when the rejected parent commits child abuse*" – holding that the trial court was entitled, on the record, to find both that the mother's abuse claims were unsupported *and* that her own conduct constituted alienation ([Colorado Lawyer summary](https://cl.cobar.org/from-the-courts/in-re-marriage-of-humphries/)).
- The Court of Appeals accepted the trial court's finding of "ample evidence of mother's alienating behaviors, based on expert and lay witness testimony, and on documentary evidence accumulated over many years" ([Colorado Lawyer summary](https://cl.cobar.org/from-the-courts/in-re-marriage-of-humphries/)).

### What the Court of Appeals *reversed*

Two of the trial court's remedies were reversed:

1. **Reallocation of sole decision-making responsibility to the father.** The trial court purported to act under Colorado Revised Statutes **§ 14-10-129.5(2)(b)** and **(2)(h)**, the general provisions empowering a court to fashion remedies for parenting-time violations. The Court of Appeals held this was an end-run around the heightened standards set by **§ 14-10-129.5(2)(f)** and **§ 14-10-131(2)**, which specifically govern modifications of *decision-making* responsibility and require specific findings on the statutory factors ([Colorado Lawyer summary](https://cl.cobar.org/from-the-courts/in-re-marriage-of-humphries/); [Justia summary](https://law.justia.com/cases/colorado/court-of-appeals/2024/22ca1327.html)). The reallocation was therefore set aside.
2. **The $36,500 civil fine.** The fine could not stand because "*the district court did not make sufficiently explicit factual findings*" to allow appellate review, and because **§ 14-10-129.5(2)(e.5)** caps any civil fine for parenting-time violations at **$100 per incident**. Without per-incident findings, the lump sum could not be sustained ([Colorado Lawyer summary](https://cl.cobar.org/from-the-courts/in-re-marriage-of-humphries/)).

### What the Court of Appeals dismissed or left open

The Court of Appeals **denied the father's request for appellate attorney fees** on the ground that the mother had prevailed on a substantial aspect of the appeal ([Colorado Lawyer summary](https://cl.cobar.org/from-the-courts/in-re-marriage-of-humphries/)). The trial-court fee award was left **stayed pending remand** for fresh consideration consistent with the appellate opinion.

The disposition was therefore mixed and turned on a series of distinct sub-issues rather than a single global reversal.

## Reactions and commentary

- The official **Colorado Lawyer** summary, published by the Colorado Bar Association, frames *Humphries* primarily as a case about the **statutory architecture of parenting-time remedies**: the central holding is that a parenting-time-violation finding cannot itself be the vehicle for a sole-decision-making transfer, and that any civil fine has to satisfy the per-incident specificity requirement ([Colorado Lawyer](https://cl.cobar.org/from-the-courts/in-re-marriage-of-humphries/)).
- **Justia's** case summary frames the alienation analysis as one of evidentiary admissibility: the appellate court rejected the categorical argument that alienation cannot coexist with claimed abuse, leaving room for trial courts to make case-by-case findings ([Justia](https://law.justia.com/cases/colorado/court-of-appeals/2024/22ca1327.html)).
- The American Bar Association's Family Law Quarterly (March 2024 issue) and similar US family-law commentary continue to treat parental-alienation expert evidence as case-by-case admissible in Colorado, with *Humphries* now serving as the leading published Colorado authority on the issue ([ABA Family Law Quarterly](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/family_law/resources/family-law-quarterly/2024-march/judicial-perceptions-parental-alienation-its-legal-remedies/)).

## Why this case matters

*Humphries* is a useful case to include in an alienation-law repository **precisely because it does not fit the simple narrative on either side**.

For the parental-alienation-affirming side, *Humphries* is a published state appellate decision that:

- accepts that expert testimony on parental alienation can be sufficiently reliable to admit;
- rejects the categorical proposition that an alienation finding is incompatible with the rejected parent's having committed abuse;
- and **affirms** an aggressively documented finding of "severe alienation" supported by extensive lay and expert evidence.

For the parental-alienation-sceptical side, *Humphries* is a published state appellate decision that:

- requires that any *remedy* imposed for parenting-time violations comply strictly with the statutory framework: a finding of alienation, however well evidenced, does not give the court a free hand to transfer decision-making responsibility outside the statutory standards;
- imposes per-incident specificity on any civil fine, ruling out lump-sum sanctions that effectively translate alienation findings into financial punishment without granular factual support; and
- denies the prevailing party (father) appellate attorney fees because the mother won meaningfully on appeal.

The case is therefore the **closest US analog to *Re Y* in spirit** – not because it shares Re Y's scepticism of alienation expertise, but because it insists on procedural and statutory discipline regardless of how compelling the trial court found the alienation evidence to be. *Re Y* polices who counts as an expert in England; *Humphries* polices what remedies a Colorado court can impose once it has accepted that expertise.

For the family-law profession, *Humphries* is also the leading current published Colorado authority on how the **federal-style** *Daubert*-adjacent reliability inquiry interacts with the trial court's gate-keeping role over alienation testimony – and confirms that this gate-keeping does not require courts to exclude that testimony as a class.

For the parental-alienation-advocacy community, the honest comparative lesson is that *Humphries* is **not** a "PA finding reversed on appeal" case in the way *Re Y* approaches the issue. It is a case where the PA finding stood, but two of the punitive remedies the trial court built on top of it did not. That nuance matters: it shows that even where appellate courts accept the substance of alienation evidence, they continue to insist on careful statutory tailoring of the consequences.

## A note on the search for a clean US "appellate reversal of a PA finding"

This page was researched alongside an explicit effort to find a US state-appellate or federal Pennsylvania decision in which an alienation-based custody change was **reversed** on appeal, symmetrical to the UK's *Re Y*. The published US appellate record is dominated either by affirmance of alienation findings (e.g. the Tennessee line discussed in *Taylor v. Taylor* (Tenn. Ct. App. 2023) and the Louisiana line considered in *Hilkirk v. Johnson* (La. Ct. App. 2015)) or by procedural-tailoring decisions like *Humphries* itself. A clean US appellate reversal of the *core* parental-alienation finding, on a published record meeting this repository's evidentiary standards, **was not located within the time budget** of this round of research. That gap is flagged honestly rather than papered over.
