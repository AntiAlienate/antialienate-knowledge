## Background

The case arises in a Spanish family-law landscape that is one of the most polarised in Europe on parental alienation. The political and ministerial position – consolidated under the 2021 *Ley Orgánica 8/2021 de protección integral a la infancia y la adolescencia frente a la violencia* (the "Ley Rhodes") – is sceptical of "síndrome de alienación parental" (SAP) as a clinical concept and is hostile to its use in custody proceedings. Successive Justice Ministry circulars and the General Council of the Judiciary (CGPJ) guidance have discouraged judicial reliance on the SAP label.

The Tribunal Supremo's own case-law, however, has been more nuanced. While the TS rarely uses the words "síndrome de alienación parental" in its own *fundamentos de derecho*, it has consistently held – in a line of sentencias of which STS 519/2017 is one of the clearest – that:

1. a child's expressed wish to exclude one parent is not, on its own, determinative of residence;
2. parents are under a legal duty not to "influir negativamente" (negatively influence) the child's perception of the other parent;
3. where the *audiencia provincial* has found, on expert and social-services evidence, that the child's rejection is the product of one parent's conduct rather than of reasoned grounds, the TS will dismiss the cassation appeal against an order that protects the child's relationship with both parents – including, in appropriate cases, by ordering shared custody.

STS 519/2017 is the leading recent example of the TS handling those issues in their PA-coded form without using the SAP label.

## Key facts

- A daughter was born to the parties in **2004**.
- After separation, the matter went to the **Juzgado de Primera Instancia of Alicante**, which awarded the father an arrangement effectively equivalent to shared parenting (the father had the daughter at his home approximately as much as the mother).
- The mother appealed. The **Audiencia Provincial de Alicante, Sección 9ª**, by sentencia of **25 April 2016**, confirmed the first-instance ruling. The Audiencia recorded that the minor had expressed a strong wish to remain with her mother and to exclude the father, but that the evidence – including reports from the *equipo psicosocial* and the *Ministerio Fiscal* – indicated that her rejection of the father was a product of maternal influence rather than of any independent reasoned ground.
- The mother lodged a *recurso de casación* before the Tribunal Supremo, arguing principally (i) that the lower courts had failed to give due weight to the daughter's expressed wishes under Article 9 of the *Ley Orgánica 1/1996, de Protección Jurídica del Menor*, and (ii) that ordering shared custody where the child was opposed was contrary to the child's *interés superior*.

## Procedural posture

A *recurso de casación* before the Sala Primera (Sala Civil) of the Tribunal Supremo, directed against the substantive sentencia of the Audiencia Provincial de Alicante, Sección 9ª, of 25 April 2016. The applicable substantive provisions were principally **Article 92 of the Código Civil** (custody arrangements following separation/divorce, including shared custody), **Article 39 of the Spanish Constitution** (protection of the family and of minors), and **Article 9 of the Ley Orgánica 1/1996** (right of the minor to be heard). The cassation analysis took place within the doctrinal framework of the TS's settled shared-custody jurisprudence as consolidated in **STS 257/2013 of 29 April 2013** (ponente José Antonio Seijas Quintana), which had established that shared custody is to be treated as the normal and even desirable arrangement, not as exceptional ([STS 257/2013 case note on vLex](https://vlex.es/vid/guarda-custodia-compartida-jurisprudencial-438316050)).

## The judgment

The Sala Primera **dismissed the mother's recurso de casación** (desestimar el recurso de casación) and confirmed the Audiencia Provincial's order continuing the shared parenting arrangement, with the residence equilibrium that the lower courts had found to be in the child's interest.

The decision is widely-quoted in practitioner literature on Spanish PA cases for two propositions:

1. **The duty not to negatively influence the child's view of the other parent.** The TS held that each parent's exercise of *patria potestad* (parental responsibility) carries with it a legal duty:

> "no influir negativamente en las opiniones de su hija, permitiéndole un armónico desarrollo de su personalidad"

(English translation: "not to negatively influence the opinions of [his/her] daughter, allowing her a harmonious development of her personality"; quoted in [Diario La Ley case-note](https://diariolaley.laleynext.es/dll/2021/03/29/relaciones-postconvivenciales-hijos-menores-manipulacion-psicologica-y-alienacion-parental).)

2. **The expressed will of a 12-year-old child is not determinative where it has no reasoned basis.** The TS held, in substance, that a minor of around twelve years of age cannot, by her own expressed will, exclude one parent from her life "sin expresar una causa razonable" – without articulating a reasonable cause – where the surrounding evidence shows that her stated wish is the product of negative influence by the other parent ([Mateo Bueno – practitioner case-note](https://www.mateobuenoabogado.com/alienacion-parental/); [Bastida Abogados – practitioner case-note](https://www.bastidabogados.com/2019/06/27/argumentos-del-tribunal-supremo-otorgar-una-custodia-compartida-casos-sindrome-alienacion-parental/)).

The Court did not use the words "síndrome de alienación parental". The TS has been consistently careful since the mid-2010s not to deploy that label in its own *fundamentos de derecho*. What it did was reaffirm – using the more neutral vocabulary of *interés superior del menor*, *influencia negativa*, *manipulación parental* and *desarrollo armónico de la personalidad* – the substantive proposition that resident-parent conduct that turns a child against the other parent cannot be allowed to dictate the custodial outcome.

### Operative part

The sentencia *desestima el recurso de casación*, with costs against the recurrente, and confirms the Audiencia Provincial's order. (The full *fallo* is available in the CENDOJ database via the poderjudicial.es search using STS 519/2017, 22 September 2017.)

## Reactions and commentary

- **Practitioner reception:** STS 519/2017 has become, in the years since, the most-cited Tribunal Supremo authority in practitioner commentary on "manipulación parental" and SAP-coded custody disputes ([Bastida Abogados](https://www.bastidabogados.com/2019/06/27/argumentos-del-tribunal-supremo-otorgar-una-custodia-compartida-casos-sindrome-alienacion-parental/); [Mateo Bueno Abogado](https://www.mateobuenoabogado.com/alienacion-parental/); [Diario La Ley 2021](https://diariolaley.laleynext.es/dll/2021/03/29/relaciones-postconvivenciales-hijos-menores-manipulacion-psicologica-y-alienacion-parental)). It is read as the leading recent statement of the principle that a child's manipulated expressed will cannot defeat the *interés superior* analysis.
- **Follow-on cassations:** the Tribunal Supremo subsequently issued further sentencias along the same line, including the widely-discussed STS of 11 April 2018 (Sala Civil), which confirmed a transfer of custody from mother to father in a case where the mother had obstructed contact (five enforcement convictions) and conducted unauthorised psychiatric evaluations of the minor ([Bastida Abogados case-note on the 11 April 2018 ruling](https://www.bastidabogados.com/2018/09/24/ultimo-caso-sindrome-alienacion-parental-sentencia-del-tribunal-supremo-1142018/)).
- **Critical reception:** the Spanish Ministry of Justice and CGPJ have consistently declined to endorse SAP terminology. The Asociación Española de Neuropsiquiatría, the Consejo General de Psicología, and significant feminist legal-policy organisations argue that PA-coded reasoning in custody disputes systematically undermines the protection of children and mothers exposed to domestic violence. Their critique culminated in the *Ley Orgánica 8/2021* ("Ley Rhodes") on integral protection of children from violence, which expressly disfavours SAP-based reasoning ([Revista Española de Medicina Legal](https://www.elsevier.es/es-revista-revista-espanola-medicina-legal-285-articulo-el-llamado-sindrome-alienacion-parental-S0377473221000559?newsletter=true); [LegalToday overview](https://www.legaltoday.com/practica-juridica/derecho-civil/familia/el-llamado-sindrome-de-alienacion-parental-sap-verdad-o-mito-2020-02-17/)).
- **DDHH Abogados / ECtHR-comparative commentary:** Spanish practitioners have also begun to frame the issue through Article 8 ECHR and the Strasbourg failure-to-enforce-contact jurisprudence, treating STS 519/2017 as the Spanish parallel ([DDHH Abogados](https://ddhh.es/alienacion-parental/)).

## Why this case matters

STS 519/2017 is the cleanest example available, at top-court level in a major civil-law European jurisdiction, of a Supreme Court doing PA work without doing PA labels.

That distinction is doctrinally important. The Tribunal Supremo is operating in a national political environment that has – through ministerial circulars, CGPJ guidance, and ultimately the 2021 Ley Rhodes – discouraged the use of "síndrome de alienación parental" in family-court proceedings. The TS has responded by keeping the *substantive* logic of alienation analysis – the duty not to negatively influence the child, the irreducibility of the child's *interés superior* to the child's expressed wish, the appropriateness of intervening custodial measures where one parent's conduct has produced the child's rejection of the other – while shedding the contested *clinical* vocabulary.

That approach has at least three implications for the wider European PA debate.

First, it shows that the *legal* concept of parental alienation can survive even where the *clinical* concept is officially disfavoured. Spanish family courts continue, post-STS 519/2017, to reverse residence and order shared custody in classic PA fact patterns, simply by reasoning in *interés superior* terms rather than in SAP terms.

Second, it offers a model for jurisdictions in which the SAP debate has become culturally toxic. The TS's vocabulary – "no influir negativamente", "desarrollo armónico de la personalidad", "manipulación parental" – is clinically neutral, evidentially defensible, and judicially portable across European civil-law systems.

Third, it sits in instructive tension with the German BVerfG 1 BvR 1076/23 (2023). The BVerfG also rejects the SAP *label* but uses that rejection to strike down a custody reversal; the Spanish TS, by contrast, sheds the SAP label and uses neutral vocabulary to *uphold* a custody arrangement protecting the relationship with the non-favoured parent. Same vocabulary discipline; opposite operative result. That contrast is the cleanest comparative illustration available of how civil-law jurisdictions can converge on rejecting SAP terminology while diverging on the underlying custodial questions.

For families and lawyers using this case, the practical takeaway is that, in Spain, the *interés superior del menor* under Article 39 CE and Article 92 CC – combined with the parental duty "no influir negativamente" articulated in STS 519/2017 – is the operative vocabulary for what other systems call "parental alienation". Anyone presenting evidence in Spanish family courts is better served by that vocabulary than by SAP terminology, even where the underlying clinical facts are identical.
