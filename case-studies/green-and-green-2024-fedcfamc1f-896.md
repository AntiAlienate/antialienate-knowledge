## Background

Green & Green is a 2024 interim-stage decision of the Federal Circuit and Family Court of Australia (Division 1) – the senior tier of the Australian federal family-court system created by the 2021 merger of the Family Court and the Federal Circuit Court.

The decision falls directly into the post-reform landscape created by the **Family Law Amendment Act 2023 (Cth)**, which from **6 May 2024** repealed the presumption of equal shared parental responsibility in s 61DA of the Family Law Act and reshaped the "best interests" considerations in s 60CC. Green is one of the early judgments published under the new framework and is now routinely cited in Australian commentary as illustrative of how the court handles competing allegations of (a) parental alienation and (b) protective concerns / family violence, in the new statutory environment ([The Legal House – 2025 guide](https://thelegalhouse.com.au/understanding-parental-alienation-australian-family-law/); [Pentana Stanton](https://pentanastanton.com.au/case-summary-parental-alienation-in-family-law-cases/)).

## Key facts

- The parents separated in January 2023.
- After separation, Y lived primarily with Ms Green. A series of interim consent orders moved Mr Green's time with Y from supervised to unsupervised over many months.
- By **October 2024**, Y had stopped spending any time with the father altogether.
- Mr Green alleged that Ms Green had **alienated** Y from him and from his extended family, and sought an **interim order changing Y's primary residence** to himself.
- Ms Green's case was that Y's resistance to contact arose from Mr Green's own conduct.
- The court was told that Mr Green was **facing concurrent criminal proceedings in relation to sexual-assault allegations** – the published reasons treat those criminal proceedings as a material factual matrix without making any criminal-standard finding. The fact that the alienation application sat against a backdrop of unresolved criminal allegations is one of the case's defining features ([Pentana Stanton summary](https://pentanastanton.com.au/case-summary-parental-alienation-in-family-law-cases/)).
- Y was reported by treating clinicians to be in **acute psychological distress**, including expressions of **suicidal ideation**.
- Mr Green's case relied in part on a parental-alienation theoretical framework; the court was alert to the warning from the Full Court in *Shell & Armel* [2022] FedCFamC1A 83 that primary judges should not import the psychological literature on alienation (e.g. the Kelly & Johnston 2001 reformulation) other than through properly tendered expert evidence ([Pentana Stanton](https://pentanastanton.com.au/case-summary-parental-alienation-in-family-law-cases/)).

## Procedural posture

An **interim** application by the father for a change of residence, against a background of contested allegations on both sides. The trial judge was therefore operating in the constrained interim-stage decisional framework laid down by the Full Court in **Banks & Banks [2015] FamCAFC 36** (2015) FLC 93-637, which restricts the use of contested factual material at interim hearings and reserves complex fact-finding for trial.

## The judgment

The court **dismissed Mr Green's interim application** to change Y's residence.

Key reasoning the published summaries draw out:

- **Mental-health priority.** The court found that Y's acute distress – including suicidal ideation – was the controlling welfare consideration and that an abrupt change of residence to a parent whom Y had stopped seeing would risk serious psychological harm.
- **Alienation finding deferred to trial.** The court accepted that the father's alienation allegations were properly raised and were not to be dismissed at the interim stage, but held they could not be **resolved** at interim. Following *Banks & Banks*, the trial judge said the competing narratives – alienation by the mother on one hand; resistance grounded in the father's own conduct on the other – required final-hearing determination.
- **Criminal-proceedings overlay.** The judgment treats the unresolved sexual-assault proceedings against Mr Green as a relevant, although not determinative, factor. The court did not treat the criminal allegations as proven; nor did it disregard them. This careful intermediate posture reflects the new s 60CC structure under the 2024 reforms, which gives primacy to the child's safety and to the benefit of relationships when safe.
- **Operational orders.** The court ordered **immediate psychological / mental-health support** for Y, and ordered that contact with Mr Green could occur **in accordance with Y's wishes** – effectively child-led contact for the interim period until final hearing.

Drawing on the published case summaries, the trial judge's key formulation in substance was that "where a child's mental health is in jeopardy and serious allegations of coercion and alienation are made, the Court must act with care" ([Pentana Stanton](https://pentanastanton.com.au/case-summary-parental-alienation-in-family-law-cases/)) – a statement of the interim-stage caution principle.

## Reactions and commentary

- **Pentana Stanton** (Australian family-law firm) treats Green & Green as the modern leading interim-stage illustration of how Australian courts navigate alienation claims against safety concerns under the 2024 reforms ([Pentana Stanton](https://pentanastanton.com.au/case-summary-parental-alienation-in-family-law-cases/)).
- **The Legal House** describes Green as part of a small cluster of 2024 cases that show the new s 60CC structure in operation, emphasising the court's willingness to take child mental-health evidence seriously and to refuse coercive remedies at interim ([The Legal House 2025 guide](https://thelegalhouse.com.au/understanding-parental-alienation-australian-family-law/)).
- The **Independent Children's Lawyer (ICL) significant-cases bulletin** cites the case (and related 2024–2025 decisions) as part of the practitioner-facing post-reform jurisprudence ([ICL Australia – March 2025 significant cases](https://icl.gov.au/news/significant-cases-published-march-2025)).
- The **Saturday Paper** investigation by **Eliza Wynn** ("The legal battlefield of parental alienation"), republished and indexed by the **Safe & Together Institute**, supplies the wider critical context: the rise of alienation framings in Australian family-law litigation and the way they can intersect with allegations of family violence and child sexual abuse ([Safe & Together Institute repost](https://safeandtogetherinstitute.com/press/the-saturday-paper-the-legal-battlefield-of-parental-alienation)).
- Academic context: **Professor Zoe Rathus AM** (Griffith Law School) has written extensively about Australian family-law reform and the parental-alienation discourse; her 2025 conference slide deck "Unpacking Family Law: Reforms, Family Violence and Parental Alienation" treats the post-reform jurisprudence (including the Green-cluster decisions) within a broader family-violence frame ([Rathus slides](https://qldflpn.org.au/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/Unpacking-Family-Law-Slides-Zoe-Rathus-1.pdf)).
- Earlier doctrinal scaffolding: **Banks & Banks [2015] FamCAFC 36** (the interim-stage caution rule); **Shell & Armel [2022] FedCFamC1A 83** (the rule that PA theoretical literature cannot be relied on at primary level without proper expert tender). Both are part of the citation chain that any responsible Australian PA discussion now travels through.

## Why this case matters

Green & Green is the post-reform Australian case that takes the parental-alienation question and refuses to answer it in the abstract. The father's narrative – that Ms Green has alienated Y – is treated as serious and worth trying at a final hearing. The mother's narrative – that Y's resistance is rationally grounded in the father's conduct, including the matter that is in the criminal court – is also treated as serious. And in between, the court does the thing the Australian family-law system was reformed in 2024 to do: it puts the 12-year-old child's actual mental-health crisis at the centre.

For Australian advocates, that combination matters. Before the 2024 reforms, the s 61DA presumption of equal shared parental responsibility was sometimes treated as a thumb on the scale toward coercive contact remedies in alienation-framed cases. Green is one of the first published interim decisions to demonstrate that, with the presumption gone, the trial judge's analytical space is larger: alienation allegations do not import a default remedy, and child-led contact under intensive mental-health support is now openly available as the holding pattern until final fact-finding.

For families, lawyers and policymakers internationally, Green is also a useful tile in the comparative mosaic. It sits naturally next to **Re Y (Experts and Alienating Behaviour) [2026] EWFC 38** (England and Wales – fact-finding before alienation experts), **L (AG) v D (KB)** (Ontario – the Canadian articulation of the same caution), and the various ECtHR Italian-cluster judgments (the human-rights overlay). Each jurisdiction has now produced a leading decision saying, in its own legal vocabulary, the same thing: alienation framing does not displace the child's voice, the child's safety, or the proper trial of contested facts.

For parents in an Australian PA-framed dispute, Green is a working answer to a common fear – that an alienation allegation alone will trigger a residence switch. It is also a working answer to a common counter-fear – that family violence and alienation cannot coexist as live issues. The court took both seriously. It just refused to resolve them at interim, and protected the child while the matter was set down properly.
