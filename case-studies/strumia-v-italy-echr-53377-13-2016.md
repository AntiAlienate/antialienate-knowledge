## Background

The application arose from the protracted breakdown of a marriage in Italy and the resulting struggle of a separated Italian father to obtain effective contact with his young daughter after the mother left the family home. The case is one of a continuous line of ECtHR judgments against Italy under Article 8 in which Strasbourg has found that the Italian domestic courts and social services systematically fail to enforce contact orders with the diligence the Convention requires. It sits alongside *Lombardo v Italy* (2013), *Bondavalli v Italy* (2015), *Improta v Italy* (2017), *Solarino v Italy* (2017) and *R.V. and Others v Italy* (2018) — together forming what Italian commentators describe as a "filone giurisprudenziale" (jurisprudential line) on the principio di bigenitorialità (principle of joint parenting).

Strumia is among the more striking of these cases because the obstruction of contact persisted for **approximately eight years** despite the criminal allegations made against the applicant being dismissed in his favour and despite repeated findings by court-appointed experts and social services that the child wished to and benefited from contact with her father.

## Key facts

- The applicant and his then-wife "N.R." had a daughter, "S.", born **11 September 2004**.
- On **1 May 2007**, N.R. left the matrimonial home with the child and went to live with her family in **Piombino** (Tuscany).
- On **21 May 2007**, N.R. applied for urgent protective measures from the **Tribunale per i minorenni di Firenze** (Juvenile Court of Florence) alleging that the child had been abused.
- On **3 July 2007**, N.R. filed a **criminal complaint for sexual violence** against the applicant.
- On **15 November 2007**, the Juvenile Court of Florence ordered that contact between the applicant and his daughter take place in a **protected environment** (incontri in ambiente protetto).
- Civil separation proceedings were heard by the **Tribunale di Pisa**; legal separation was pronounced in **2010**. The decision was subsequently reformed by the **Corte d'Appello di Firenze** (Florence Court of Appeal).
- Across approximately eight years of proceedings, three parallel tracks ran: (A) civil proceedings on custody and contact; (B) criminal proceedings against the applicant on the abuse allegations; and (C) criminal proceedings against N.R. The criminal allegations against the applicant did not result in conviction, and social services and successive expert assessments documented that the child had a positive disposition toward her father and would benefit from contact.
- Notwithstanding favourable expert reports and successive court orders, the protected meetings repeatedly failed to take place or were ineffective because of the resident parent's obstruction and the slow, often passive response of social services.
- By **25 February 2014**, the Florence Court of Appeal had reached the point of **suspending the mother's parental authority** in light of her continued obstruction — but the contact relationship had by then been substantially destroyed.

## Procedural posture

A Chamber judgment of the **First Section** of the ECtHR. The applicant complained exclusively under **Article 8 ECHR** (right to respect for private and family life), arguing that the Italian state had failed to take adequate, prompt and effective measures to give legal force to his contact rights with his daughter.

## The judgment

The Court found **a violation of Article 8** of the Convention.

The Court applied its settled positive-obligations test: where a parent and child's right to family life is in issue, the State has an obligation to take "all necessary measures that can reasonably be required" to facilitate the reunion of parent and child, and proceedings concerning contact and custody require **exceptional diligence** because the passage of time can have irreversible consequences on the relationship between a child and the parent with whom they do not live.

The Italian-language version published by the Italian Ministry of Justice records the Court's central finding in these terms:

> "*le autorità nazionali non abbiano fatto sforzi adeguati e sufficienti per far rispettare il diritto di visita del ricorrente*"
> ("the national authorities did not make adequate and sufficient efforts to enforce the applicant's right of contact") — [Giustizia.it](https://www.giustizia.it/giustizia/it/mg_1_20_1.page?facetNode_1=1_2%282016%29&facetNode_2=1_2%28201606%29&facetNode_3=1_2%2820160623%29&facetNode_4=0_8_1_4&contentId=SDU1269983&previsiousPage=mg_1_20)

and concludes:

> "*vi è stata violazione dell'articolo 8 della Convenzione*"
> ("there has been a violation of Article 8 of the Convention") — [Giustizia.it](https://www.giustizia.it/giustizia/it/mg_1_20_1.page?facetNode_1=1_2%282016%29&facetNode_2=1_2%28201606%29&facetNode_3=1_2%2820160623%29&facetNode_4=0_8_1_4&contentId=SDU1269983&previsiousPage=mg_1_20)

The Court emphasised that the cumulative effect of the State's passivity — protected meetings ordered but not effectively held; social-services interventions ordered but not delivered; expert recommendations not translated into binding action — was what produced the violation, not any single bad-faith act by an individual court or service. Italian authorities were found to have **tolerated the resident parent's unilateral obstruction** of the father–daughter relationship.

**Just satisfaction (Article 41):** the Italian-language summary records that the Court awarded the applicant **€15,000 for non-pecuniary damage** ("*la Corte accorda all'interessato la somma di 15.000 EUR*" — [Giustizia.it](https://www.giustizia.it/giustizia/it/mg_1_20_1.page?facetNode_1=1_2%282016%29&facetNode_2=1_2%28201606%29&facetNode_3=1_2%2820160623%29&facetNode_4=0_8_1_4&contentId=SDU1269983&previsiousPage=mg_1_20)). The award for costs and expenses is **not extracted on this page** — please refer to the HUDOC judgment for the full operative part.

- The ECtHR's own **Italy country press factsheet** lists Strumia among Italian Article 8 family-life cases ([ECHR Italy factsheet](https://www.echr.coe.int/documents/d/echr/cp_italy_eng)).
- The **Centro di Ateneo per i Diritti Umani** (University of Padua) catalogues Strumia in its index of Italian Article 8 violations ([Padua human rights centre index](https://unipd-centrodirittiumani.it/en/topics/european-court-of-human-rights-judgements-against-italy-on-violations-of-article-8-echr-part-1)).
- In Italian academic and practitioner writing on the **principio di bigenitorialità**, Strumia is regularly cited alongside *Lombardo*, *Bondavalli*, *Improta*, *Solarino* and *R.V. and Others* as Strasbourg authority that delay and passivity in enforcing the non-resident parent's contact rights is itself a Convention violation.
- In the parental-alienation literature, Strumia is frequently invoked as among the clearest examples in which a state's failure to act allowed the relationship between a child and a parent to be eroded over almost a decade. Care should be taken: the judgment itself does **not** use the term "parental alienation" or endorse any particular psychological framework — its operative reasoning is institutional (state diligence) rather than diagnostic.

## Why this case matters

Strumia is the case in the Italian Article 8 cluster where the duration of the harm is most visible: roughly **eight years** during which a father pursued contact with his daughter while criminal allegations against him collapsed, experts and social services repeatedly recommended contact, and yet the protected meetings ordered by the Juvenile Court of Florence in November 2007 never developed into a meaningful, ongoing parent–child relationship. By the time the Florence Court of Appeal suspended the mother's parental authority in 2014, the relationship the contact regime had been designed to protect had largely been destroyed.

For parental-alienation advocacy and for any family-law system that is sluggish in enforcing contact, the doctrinal takeaway is the same as in *Improta* but written across a longer and more punishing timeline: where a resident parent unilaterally obstructs the non-resident parent's relationship with a child, the State is not a neutral bystander. Article 8 imposes a positive obligation to act with exceptional diligence; failure to do so engages the State's responsibility.

For the parental-alienation movement specifically, Strumia also offers a sober corrective. The Court does not adjudicate "alienation" as a clinical syndrome. It adjudicates the failure of institutions — juvenile courts, civil courts, courts of appeal, social services, court-appointed experts — to translate orders into a real relationship. That framing is more durable than any diagnostic label and is increasingly the lever by which European-level claims against contact obstruction succeed.
