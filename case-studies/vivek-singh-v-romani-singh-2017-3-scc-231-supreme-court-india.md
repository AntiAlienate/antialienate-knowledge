# Vivek Singh v Romani Singh, Civil Appeal No. 3962 of 2016, (2017) 3 SCC 231 (Supreme Court of India, 13 February 2017)

**Court:** Supreme Court of India (Bench of two Judges)
**Date:** 13 February 2017
**Bench:** Justice **J. Chelameswar** and Justice **A.K. Sikri** (judgment delivered by Sikri J)
**Citation:** (2017) 3 SCC 231; Civil Appeal No. 3962 of 2016 ([Supreme Court of India – judgment PDF on main.sci.gov.in](https://main.sci.gov.in/judgment/judis/44566.pdf))
**Reported at:** [Supreme Court Cases summary](https://www.supremecourtcases.com/vivek-singh-v-romani-singh-3/) | [CaseMine entry](https://www.casemine.com/judgement/in/5de46f2c46571b63ad4f02ec) | [LiveLaw – "Supreme Court Recognises Parental Alienation Syndrome"](https://www.livelaw.in/supreme-court-recognises-parental-alienation-syndrome) | [Mondaq commentary](https://www.mondaq.com/india/family-law/605916/parental-alienation-syndrome-in-the-light-of-vivek-singh-vs-romani-singh-case)

## Parties

- **Appellant:** **Lt Col Vivek Singh**, Indian Army officer, father of the minor child. Senior Counsel: **V. Shekhar** ([ShoneeKapoor case summary](https://www.shoneekapoor.com/vivek-singh-vs-romani-singh/)).
- **Respondent:** **Romani Singh**, teacher at Kendriya Vidyalaya, mother of the minor child. Senior Counsel: **Geeta Luthra** ([ShoneeKapoor case summary](https://www.shoneekapoor.com/vivek-singh-vs-romani-singh/)).
- **Minor child:** **Saesha Singh**, born **29 October 2008**. The child's identity is recorded in the publicly accessible commentary on the judgment; the Supreme Court's order is published on its public website ([SCI PDF](https://main.sci.gov.in/judgment/judis/44566.pdf)).
- **Court Counsellor (Family Court, Patiala House):** **Ms Iti Kanungo**, whose report on the child's preference for her father was discussed in the judgment ([ShoneeKapoor case summary](https://www.shoneekapoor.com/vivek-singh-vs-romani-singh/)).

## Background

India does not yet have an explicit statutory framework recognising parental alienation. Custody disputes are decided under the **Guardians and Wards Act 1890** and, for Hindu families, under **section 26 of the Hindu Marriage Act 1955**, with the welfare of the child as the paramount consideration. The country's appellate jurisprudence on the psychological dynamics of post-separation conflict is correspondingly thin.

*Vivek Singh v Romani Singh* is significant because it is the first reported judgment of the Supreme Court of India to use the language of "parental alienation syndrome" as part of its operative reasoning in a custody appeal. The Court did not adopt the term as a formal diagnostic category. It did, however, draw on psychological literature — explicitly citing the work of Dr Richard A. Gardner — to explain why a child's expressed preference for one parent, formed during years of physical custody with that parent and during a period of conflict between the parents, could not be treated as a transparent indicator of the child's welfare.

## Key facts

- The parties married on **25 November 2007**.
- Their daughter Saesha was born on **29 October 2008**.
- Following matrimonial discord, the mother left the matrimonial home on **4 August 2010**, when the child was approximately **21 months old**. The father retained physical custody from that point.
- The mother immediately commenced guardianship proceedings under the Guardians and Wards Act 1890 (GP No. 43 of 2010).
- On **7 December 2011**, the **Family Court at Patiala House, New Delhi** dismissed the mother's petition and continued custody with the father.
- On **2 April 2013**, the **Delhi High Court** allowed the mother's appeal (FAO No. 39 of 2012), granting custody to the mother and providing for the father's visitation.
- The father did not implement the High Court's order. On **1 July 2013**, when the Supreme Court issued notice on the father's special leave petition, it stayed contempt proceedings, leaving the child with the father pending appeal.
- By the time the Supreme Court heard the matter on the merits, the child had lived continuously with her father from age 21 months to over **8 years old**.
- A Family Court Counsellor's interaction with the child recorded that she expressed a strong preference to remain with her father.

## Procedural posture

A civil appeal to the Supreme Court of India under Article 136 of the Constitution (Special Leave Petition converted to Civil Appeal No. 3962 of 2016) from the order of the Delhi High Court in FAO No. 39 of 2012 ([Supreme Court PDF](https://main.sci.gov.in/judgment/judis/44566.pdf)). The Supreme Court was sitting in its appellate civil jurisdiction.

## The judgment

The judgment was delivered by **Sikri J** for the two-Judge Bench on **13 February 2017** and is reported as **(2017) 3 SCC 231**.

The Court dismissed the father's appeal and upheld the Delhi High Court's order granting custody to the mother, modifying its operation to a graduated arrangement under which the mother was to take custody for at least one academic year, with the child to be admitted to **Kendriya Vidyalaya, INA Colony, New Delhi** (where the mother taught), and with the matter to be reviewed in March 2018 ([ShoneeKapoor case summary](https://www.shoneekapoor.com/vivek-singh-vs-romani-singh/)).

The doctrinal core of the judgment, and the reason it matters for parental-alienation jurisprudence, is the Court's treatment of the child's expressed preference for her father. The Court refused to treat that preference at face value, reasoning that the child had been in the father's sole company from the age of 21 months for approximately eight years and could not, in that context, fairly assess her own welfare. The Court framed this directly through the language of **Parental Alienation Syndrome**, drawing on **Dr Richard A. Gardner's** psychiatric writing on the subject ([Mondaq commentary](https://www.mondaq.com/india/family-law/605916/parental-alienation-syndrome-in-the-light-of-vivek-singh-vs-romani-singh-case)).

LiveLaw and the practitioner commentaries record the Court's description of PAS in these terms — as "a condition where the custodial parent indoctrinates a child against the other parent, who comes to be regarded as **'totally to blame for all problems'** and as **'devoid of any positive characteristic'**" ([LiveLaw](https://www.livelaw.in/supreme-court-recognises-parental-alienation-syndrome)).

A representative passage of the Court's own reasoning, as reported in the case summaries:

> "A child, who has not seen, experienced or lived the comfort of the company of the mother is, naturally, not in a position to comprehend that the grass on the other side may turn out to be greener." — Sikri J ([ShoneeKapoor case summary](https://www.shoneekapoor.com/vivek-singh-vs-romani-singh/))

The Court identified two specific harms produced by alienating dynamics: a **loyalty conflict** that places the child in an impossible emotional position whichever parent's company she keeps, and a **distortion of reality** in which the alienating parent is presented as without fault and the targeted parent as without virtue ([ShoneeKapoor case summary](https://www.shoneekapoor.com/vivek-singh-vs-romani-singh/) | [Mondaq commentary](https://www.mondaq.com/india/family-law/605916/parental-alienation-syndrome-in-the-light-of-vivek-singh-vs-romani-singh-case)).

Operating on that analysis, the Court held that the welfare-of-the-child principle could not be satisfied by allowing the unlawful retention of the child with the father to harden into a settled arrangement. It rejected the argument that the child's eight-year residence with the father created an entitlement to remain there. It awarded custody to the mother for a trial period of one academic year with structured paternal visitation and provision for review ([Supreme Court PDF](https://main.sci.gov.in/judgment/judis/44566.pdf)).

The Court was careful, in later phrasing reported by Indian commentators, that PAS could not be "labelled" without specific instances of alienating conduct — a point developed further in subsequent Indian decisions but already foreshadowed in this judgment's emphasis on the concrete history of custody since the child was 21 months old ([Lawbeat – "Parental alienation syndrome can't be labelled sans specific instances: SC"](https://lawbeat.in/supreme-court-judgments/parental-alienation-syndrome-cant-be-labelled-sans-specific-instances-supreme-court)).

## Who else was involved

- **The Family Court at Patiala House, New Delhi** (judgment of 7 December 2011) — the trial court that dismissed the mother's guardianship petition.
- **The Delhi High Court** (FAO No. 39 of 2012, judgment of 2 April 2013) — the appellate court that granted custody to the mother and whose order the Supreme Court ultimately upheld with modifications.
- **Senior Advocate V. Shekhar** for the appellant; **Senior Advocate Geeta Luthra** for the respondent ([ShoneeKapoor case summary](https://www.shoneekapoor.com/vivek-singh-vs-romani-singh/)).
- **Ms Iti Kanungo**, Family Court Counsellor at Patiala House, whose report on the child's expressed preference was central to the Supreme Court's analysis of why expressed preferences cannot, in alienating contexts, be taken at face value.
- **Kendriya Vidyalaya, INA Colony, New Delhi** — the school at which the Supreme Court directed the mother to enrol the child as part of the transitional arrangement.

## Reactions and commentary

- **LiveLaw** reported the judgment under the headline "Supreme Court Recognises Parental Alienation Syndrome", treating it as the first Indian apex-court engagement with PAS in a custody context ([LiveLaw](https://www.livelaw.in/supreme-court-recognises-parental-alienation-syndrome)).
- **Mondaq** published a practitioner-facing analysis describing the judgment as the "semantic debut" of PAS in Indian custody jurisprudence and tracing the Court's reliance on Dr Richard A. Gardner's writing on PAS, including his 1985 paper *Recent Developments in Child Custody Litigation* in *The Academy Forum* ([Mondaq commentary](https://www.mondaq.com/india/family-law/605916/parental-alienation-syndrome-in-the-light-of-vivek-singh-vs-romani-singh-case)).
- **SCC OnLine** later reported in 2024 that the same parties' custody dispute had been finally settled by the Supreme Court after roughly nine years of litigation, with the Court again discussing "the seriousness of allegations of parental alienation syndrome" ([SCC OnLine blog, 12 June 2024](https://www.scconline.com/blog/post/2024/06/12/supreme-court-settles-child-custody-battle-after-9-yrs/)).
- **Lawbeat** has reported subsequent Supreme Court guidance that PAS cannot be "labelled sans specific instances" — read by Indian practitioners as a refinement of Vivek Singh, requiring concrete evidence of alienating conduct rather than reliance on the diagnostic label alone ([Lawbeat](https://lawbeat.in/supreme-court-judgments/parental-alienation-syndrome-cant-be-labelled-sans-specific-instances-supreme-court)).
- The judgment is now routinely cited in High Court family-law decisions, in academic notes (e.g. [Aishwarya Sandeep, "Recognition of Parental Alienation Syndrome: Vivek Singh v. Romani Singh"](https://aishwaryasandeep.com/2022/01/10/recognition-of-parental-alienation-syndrome-vivek-singh-v-romani-singh/)) and in CaseMine's editorial commentary on welfare-of-child custody jurisprudence ([CaseMine commentary](https://www.casemine.com/commentary/in/vivek-singh-v.-romani-singh:-supreme-court-reinforces-welfare-of-child-as-paramount-in-custody-disputes/view)).

## Why this case matters

For India — the world's most populous democracy and a jurisdiction with a vast and underserved family-law caseload — Vivek Singh v Romani Singh is the first Supreme Court judgment that puts the phrase "parental alienation syndrome" into operative use in a custody appeal. It does so cautiously: the Court does not treat PAS as a self-applying diagnostic test and later Indian decisions have made clear that the label is not a substitute for evidence of alienating conduct. But it commits the apex court to two propositions that matter enormously in any PA-adjacent dispute.

First, **a child's expressed preference is not dispositive** where that preference has been formed during years of physical custody with one parent, in the absence of the other, and against the backdrop of inter-parental conflict. The Court explicitly refused to allow Saesha's understandable preference for the only parent she had known to govern the outcome.

Second, **the lawful position cannot be frozen by the unlawful retention of a child**. The father had defied the Delhi High Court's order; the Supreme Court refused to let the resulting "settled" arrangement become an entitlement. That principle — that a parent who maintains de facto custody in breach of court orders does not thereby acquire a right to it — has obvious doctrinal force in any alienation case where one parent's obstruction has produced a fait accompli.

For the broader parental-alienation movement, Vivek Singh is also important because of its method. The Court did not adopt PAS as a clinical syndrome. It used it as a frame for thinking about the **distortion of a child's perception over time under sole-custody conditions**. That framing — institutional and contextual rather than diagnostic — is one that travels well across legal systems and is harder to dismiss as junk science.

For Indian families dealing with alienating conduct, the case is the doctrinal foothold that subsequent High Court decisions have built on. It puts the Supreme Court on record, in a published reported judgment, that the phenomenon the parental-alienation literature describes is real enough, and harmful enough, to justify reversing eight years of physical custody.
