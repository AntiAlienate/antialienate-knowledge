# Lombardo v Italy, ECHR App. 25704/11 (29 January 2013)

**Court:** European Court of Human Rights (Cour européenne des droits de l'homme), Second Section / Deuxième Section

## Parties

- **Applicant:** Sergio Lombardo, Italian national born in 1939, resident in Rome. Confirmed in ECHR press release ECHR 030 (2013).
- **Respondent State:** Italian Republic, represented before the Court by its Agent.
- **Child:** the applicant's daughter (name redacted by the Court in standard family-life practice). Date of birth and current age **not publicly confirmed** in the press release; consult judgment file for procedural detail.

## Background

Sergio Lombardo and the child's mother separated and proceedings before the Italian juvenile/family jurisdiction resulted in a 2003 decision fixing his contact (right of access) with his daughter. According to the ECHR press release, from the date of that 2003 order onward the applicant was unable to exercise contact under the conditions the courts had set, because:

- the mother opposed contact and obstructed handovers; and
- the social services (servizi sociali) to whom enforcement was entrusted did not take steps capable of overcoming the mother's objections; and
- the domestic courts did not act effectively to enforce their own orders.

Source: ECHR Registrar's press release 030 (2013), 29 January 2013, summarising the Lombardo facts under Article 8.

Lombardo lodged an application with the European Court of Human Rights under Article 34 of the Convention. The application was registered as no. 25704/11 (the "/11" indicating registration in 2011).

## Key facts

Drawn from the ECHR press release and from the case-law database summary (HUDOC):

- **2003** – an Italian court order fixes the applicant's contact rights with his daughter.
- The mother repeatedly refuses to comply with the contact arrangements.
- The applicant turns to social services and to the domestic courts to enforce the order; the social services prove unable or unwilling to overcome the mother's resistance, and the courts fail to take effective enforcement measures.
- A substantial period elapses (a decade between the 2003 order and the 2013 Strasbourg judgment) during which the father-daughter relationship is in practice prevented.
- **2011** – application 25704/11 lodged in Strasbourg.
- **29 January 2013** – Second Section judgment finding a violation of Article 8 ECHR (right to respect for family life).

The applicant's complaint, in the words of the ECHR press release, was that he was "unable to exercise his right of contact with his daughter under the conditions determined by the courts in 2003," because of the social services' inaction in the face of the mother's opposition and the domestic courts' inability to enforce contact arrangements.

## Procedural posture

Italian domestic proceedings 2003 onward: applicant repeatedly sought enforcement of his contact rights through the juvenile / family jurisdiction and through social services tasked with supervising contact; without practical result.

ECHR registration: application no. 25704/11 (2011).
ECHR allocation: Second Section, sitting as a Chamber under Article 26 § 1.
Notice given to the Italian Government; written observations exchanged; no oral hearing publicly indicated (Chamber procedure).
Judgment delivered 29 January 2013 (in French only).
Final pursuant to Article 44 § 2 (no referral request to the Grand Chamber granted) on 29 April 2013.

## The judgment

**Finding:** Violation of Article 8 of the Convention (right to respect for private and family life).

**Core reasoning** (as summarised in the ECHR Registrar's press release and contemporaneous commentary):

> "The Court emphasised the positive obligations inherent in ensuring enjoyment of the right to private and family life and highlighted the importance of timely action by the State when the parent–child relationship is at issue." (Centre for Global Law and Justice, summary of ECHR press release, 6 February 2013: https://cglj.org/2013/02/06/ecthr-confirms-childs-interest-in-establishing-paternity-parents-right-to-enforcement-of-visitation/)

The Second Section held that Italian authorities had **failed to act with the speed and the diligence required to restore the applicant's enjoyment of contact** with his daughter. The State's positive obligation under Article 8 includes not merely refraining from interference with family life but actively deploying enforcement tools — including coercive measures against an obstructive resident parent — when ordinary social-services mediation has failed. The passage of years without effective enforcement allowed the parent-child relationship to deteriorate to the point of being effectively destroyed, which the Court treated as a consequence attributable to the State.

The judgment sits squarely in the Court's *Italian bigenitorialità* line: the State's positive duties under Article 8 to make contact arrangements work in practice, not merely to issue paper orders. It is repeatedly cited alongside **Piazzi v Italy** (App. 36168/09, 2 November 2010), **Bondavalli v Italy** (App. 35532/12, 17 November 2015), **Improta v Italy** (App. 66396/14, 4 May 2017 — see [improta-v-italy-echr-66396-14-2017.md](improta-v-italy-echr-66396-14-2017.md)) and **Strumia v Italy** (App. 53377/13, 23 June 2016 — see [strumia-v-italy-echr-53377-13-2016.md](strumia-v-italy-echr-53377-13-2016.md)).

## Why this case matters

Lombardo is one of the cleanest single-applicant illustrations of the Court's "**positive obligations under Article 8**" doctrine applied to parental alienation patterns avant la lettre — though the Court itself does not use the label "parental alienation." The pattern the Court sanctioned is the textbook one:

1. court orders contact;
2. resident parent refuses;
3. social services try mediation, fail;
4. court issues further orders, no coercive enforcement;
5. years pass; the non-resident parent's relationship with the child is destroyed in fact.

The Court holds that this sequence — even if every individual State actor was acting in apparent good faith — constitutes a State failure under Article 8 because the cumulative effect was the destruction of family life. The State, not merely the obstructive parent, bears responsibility. This is a doctrinal lever that PA-victim parents in Convention states have used (and continue to use) to challenge welfare-led, non-coercive enforcement regimes that allow alienation to crystallise through delay.

For the AntiAlienate knowledge base, Lombardo (2013) anchors the chronological start of the modern ECHR Italian-cluster line; **Strumia v Italy** (2016) and **Improta v Italy** (2017) — both already covered in the repo — sit downstream of it. The three together establish that Italy's enforcement deficit is structural, not incidental.

The case is also notable for its applicant: born in 1939, Lombardo was already in his sixties when contact was ordered in 2003 and in his seventies when Strasbourg ruled. The Court did not treat advanced age as reducing the State's obligation to enforce.
